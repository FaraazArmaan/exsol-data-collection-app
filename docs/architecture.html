ExSol Suite

A unified multi-tenant SaaS platform that produces seven distinct customer-facing products from a single codebase and shared data model: an internal ERP, a data-collection onboarding app, a booking system, an ecommerce storefront, an in-store POS, a catalog showcase site, and per-brand portfolio sites. This document covers the High-Level and Low-Level System Design as they exist on the deployed codebase.

Stack: React 18 · Vite · TypeScript · Netlify Functions v2 · Neon Postgres · One repo, seven outputs

For a more accessible, in-depth walkthrough (suitable for non-developer readers and longer onboarding), see architecture (expansive).

The Suite

Seven products, one codebase

The whole platform is a single Git repo. From that one tree, the build produces multiple shippable products that share authentication, data, code, and operational tooling. The architectural payoff is that a fix to (say) the product catalog improves the ecommerce storefront, the POS, the catalog site, AND the data-collection onboarding flow simultaneously — because they all read from the same module. As of this revision, all seven products are live — the last three (Data Collection, Catalog, Brand Portfolio) shipped as v1s in one public-surface wave (migrations 061–062).

The relationship between Internal App and the customer-facing products
The Internal App is where data lives — it is the source of truth for products, customers, bookings, sales, files, hours, perms. The other six products are views into that data: the Ecommerce Storefront is the customer view of the Product Manager's catalog; the Catalog site is a no-purchase view of the same; the Booking System is two views (one for the staff calendar, one for the customer widget). Internally, a single products table feeds the storefront, POS, catalog, and the Ecommerce ERP module.
The Internal App

13 ERP Modules

The Internal App is structured around 13 ERP modules — the same set as a traditional enterprise resource planning system. Each module owns its own data, its own permission keys, its own UI route, and its own backend helpers. They are interconnected (the Ecommerce module reads from Order Management, which reads from Inventory, which reads from Procurement, etc.) but they are independently deployable: a workspace can have any subset enabled via the client_enabled_products table. The honest arithmetic as of this revision: 11 live (v1 or better) + 2 partial-but-functional = 13. The two partials are deliberate reuse, not absence: Order Management runs on the POS sales table today, and HR runs on the AMS roles + access-levels foundation. Width is complete — the build has switched to the depth phase (deepening modules one slice at a time; the first slice, Workforce Timesheets, is already live).

1. Finance

Books, P&L, AR/AP, payment ledgers. v1: finance_expenses ledger + a monthly P&L computed live over sales, bookings, and expenses (no revenue copy). live (v1)

2. Procurement

Purchase orders, supplier catalog, RFQs. v1: suppliers + a purchase-order FSM (draft → ordered → received) whose receive step writes stock into Inventory atomically. live (v1)

3. Manufacturing

BOMs, work orders, production runs. v1: BOMs + a production-order FSM (planned → in_progress → done); completing an order consumes components and produces finished goods atomically through the Inventory movements ledger (type='production'). live (v1)

4. Inventory Management

Stock levels, transfers, reorder points. v1: inventory_stock + an append-only stock_movements ledger; POS fulfilment decrements stock automatically. live (v1)

5. Order Management

Order intake (in-store, online, phone), fulfillment, returns. Today: the POS sales table is the partial implementation. live (partial)

6. Warehouse Management

Pick lists, putaway, bin/lot tracking. v1: named locations + per-location stock + net-zero transfers, layered over the Inventory ledger. live (v1)

7. Supply Chain Management

Cross-module flow: procurement → manufacturing → inventory → orders. v1: a read-projection dashboard — three read-only endpoints aggregating open POs, in-progress production, low stock and 30-day movement volume. Zero new tables, zero migration: the Analytics pattern applied to the supply chain. live (v1)

8. Customer Relationship Management (CRM)

Contacts, leads, opportunities, communication history. v1: a unified customer read-model — crm_customers deduped from sales + bookings — plus staff notes and an activity timeline. live (v1)

9. Project Service Resource Management

Project tracking, billable hours, resource allocation. v1: projects (quoted → active → done) linked to CRM customers, with booking-resource assignments — shipped with Workforce (059). live (v1)

10. Workforce Management

Shifts, timesheets, attendance. v1: weekly shifts laid over Booking's named resources (workforce_shifts), optionally tied to team user_nodes — plus the platform's first depth slice: timesheet_entries (107), logging and approving billable time against the same resources. live (v1 + depth)

11. Human Resources Management

Roles, levels, payroll, leave. The access-levels matrix + roles tables are the foundation. partial

12. Ecommerce

The internal half of the customer storefront — product visibility flags, channel routing, payment-on-pickup configuration. Powers /menu/<slug>. live

13. Marketing Automation

Email campaigns over the CRM read-model. v1 (060): marketing_campaigns (draft → sent) with audience selection (all / recent_30d over crm_customers), sending through the mailer seam; every recipient recorded in campaign_sends. Drip flows and segmentation are the depth follow-up. live (v1)

Width complete — the depth phase has begun
Every row above is real, enable-able software: 11 modules at v1 or better, 2 running on deliberately shared foundations (Order Management on POS sales; HR on AMS access-levels). The strategy now inverts: instead of new modules, each existing module gains depth slices — Workforce Timesheets (migration 107) is the first. Payments remains the one held design (spec ready, migration number 051 reserved, Razorpay keys pending).
Non-ERP modules that live in the Internal App
Five modules exist in the Internal App but aren't part of the 13-ERP set because they're operational rather than business-function: POS (the cashier register UI for Order Management); Booking (calendar + customer widget); File Manager (document vault with quotas + bulk ops); Analytics (read-only cross-module KPIs); and Email & Notifications (the transactional mailer seam + email_outbox behind booking confirmations and storefront receipts). They share the same auth + tenant infra as the ERP modules.
Overview

What we are building, restated

ExSol is a per-workspace operational platform. Each Client (e.g. papa-s-saloon) gets an isolated workspace inside the Internal App with team management, file storage, product catalog, point-of-sale, and booking. The Internal App is the operator surface; the six customer-facing products are configurable views into the same data. Two main authentication audiences inside the Internal App: a cross-workspace Admin console for operational oversight, and a per-workspace Owner / Staff portal for day-to-day work. Customer-facing products bypass auth entirely via opt-in tenant flags.

Modules in production

  • File Manager — Phase A live
  • Product Manager — full CRUD + CSV/XLSX import/export + 6 platform exports
  • POS v1 + v2 — staff register (FSM-gated) + public guest-checkout storefront
  • Booking & Calendar — staff calendar + public widget
  • Analytics — read-only cross-module KPIs
  • Inventory · Finance · Procurement · Warehouse — first ERP v1 wave (migrations 053–057)
  • CRM · Manufacturing · Workforce + Project Service — second ERP v1 wave (migrations 055, 058–059)
  • Data Collection · Catalog · Brand Portfolio — public-surface wave (migrations 061–062); all 7 products now live
  • Supply Chain · Marketing Automation — third wave: read-projection dashboard (no migration) + campaigns over CRM (060); all 13 ERP modules now live
  • Email & Notifications — mailer seam + outbox; booking confirmations, storefront receipts, and now campaign sends

Depth phase & held work

  • Workforce Timesheets — first depth slice live (migration 107): log/approve billable time over booking resources
  • Depth backlog — per-module deepening slices, one reserved migration range per module (see §2.7)
  • Payments — held: design spec ready (051 reserved), Razorpay keys pending; nothing live requires it

Cross-cutting capabilities

  • JWT-based bucket-user sessions
  • Google OAuth (admin + invite)
  • Workspace data export (JSON + ZIP-of-CSVs)
  • Audit trail on every mutation
  • RBAC matrix per client level
  • Transactional email (outbox-first; provider key env-gated, log-only dev fallback)
Part 1

High-Level System Design

The macro picture: layers of the system, what they own, and how requests flow through them.

1.1 System Context §context

ExSol sits between end-users on the open web and a managed Postgres backend, with Netlify acting as both CDN and serverless function host. There is no separate API server, no Kubernetes cluster, no message bus — the entire backend is a flat set of HTTP-triggered functions.

Actors

  • End-users (Owners, Staff) — log into a workspace at /c/<slug>
  • Admins — operational console at / (root)
  • Public guests (POS v2) — checkout at /c/<slug>/menu/:slug

External services

  • Google OAuth — sign-in + workspace invite
  • Netlify Blobs — image storage with thumbnail pipeline
  • Resend — transactional email via raw fetch; RESEND_API_KEY env-gated with a log-only dev fallback
  • Razorpay (held — keys pending) — payment HMAC webhook seam staged in schema

1.4 Folder Structure §folder-structure

The HLSD view of the repository: what lives where, and why. Each rule is stated first as a transferable design principle, then shown as ExSol applies it — this section doubles as a template for structuring future projects.

Principle — the top level of a repo should scream what the system IS
App, API, schema, tests, docs — identifiable from ls alone, before any code is read. Every top-level entry answers exactly one question about the system; there is no misc/, no old/, no mystery folder.

netlify/functions/ Netlify Functions v2 — 138 flat .ts endpoints (+28 module-scoped _*.ts helpers) + _shared/ server helpers: db, session, permissions, audit, mailer, exporters. Registry imports go through the @registry/* path alias, not deep-relative paths. What can the system do?

Principle — shared infrastructure stays small and boring

src/lib/ holds only what every module needs: router, auth context, API client, theme. The moment something is needed by only some modules, it belongs in a module (or modules/shared/). A growing "utils" layer is where feature-first structure goes to die.

Principle — let platform constraints shape the layout

netlify/functions/ is flat because Netlify treats a subdirectory as ONE function. Convention: <module>-<action>.ts per endpoint; the _ prefix (_shared/, _pos-authz.ts) marks "helper, not an endpoint" — skipped by routing, obvious to humans.

1.5 Load-Bearing Files & How They Connect §load-bearing

The LLSD view: the ~20 files that carry the architecture — each with a single responsibility — and the four flows that connect them. Everything else in the repo is leaf code hanging off these.

Principle — a system is legible when you can name the files that carry it
If you can't write down the short list of files where the architecture lives, you don't have an architecture — you have a pile. Keeping the list short, and each file on it to one responsibility, is a design activity, not documentation. When a load-bearing file grows a second job, split it.

Cross-module write seams

Principle — one write pattern, many sources
When several modules must affect one fact (stock on hand; mail leaving the system), give the fact a single append-only ledger and make every module write through the same pattern. Readers then trust one table instead of reconciling N side-effects.
File / tableSingle responsibilityConnections

The four connecting flows

Every meaningful behavior of the system is one of these four flows running through the files above.

Flow A — SPA route → gate · Principle: the client-side gate is UX, not security
Its whole job is to never show a link or screen the server would reject. Deleting every client gate wouldn't open a hole — it would just be a slower, uglier way to see 403s.

    → RouteMount gate(perm):         loading → session → enabledModules ∋ module

Sidebar / useNavItems gate links with the SAME data → nav and guards agree.
FE authz state comes entirely from GET /api/u-me (fetched once on mount).
Flow B — API request → DB · Principle: the server re-derives authorization from the database on every request
The JWT proves who you are; the database says what you may do, right now. A stale or tampered claim can name a user but can never grant a permission.

1.6 Multi-Tenancy §multitenancy

Every meaningful table carries client_id and is scoped at query time. There is no Postgres Row-Level Security — tenancy is enforced in application code via the auth helper layer.

Two session kinds

  • admin — global across all clients; identified by admins.id; cross-client by definition.
  • bucket_user — pinned to a single client_id in the JWT; cannot reach other clients even with a forged URL.

Cross-client guard

resolveClientId(session, req) checks the URL's ?client=<uuid> param against the session's pinned client. Bucket-user mismatch → 403 forbidden_cross_client. Admins must explicitly pass a ?client=; bucket-users may not.

Request resolution rules

// Pseudo — netlify/functions/_shared/resolve-workspace.ts (planned for v2)
export async function resolveWorkspace(req: Request): Promise<{ slug: string, clientId: string }> {
  const host = req.headers.get('host') ?? '';
  const url  = new URL(req.url);

  // 1. Custom domain? Look up alias list
  if (!host.endsWith('exsoldatacollectionapp.netlify.app') && !host.endsWith('.exsol.app')) {
    const [c] = await sql`SELECT id, slug FROM clients WHERE ${host} = ANY(domain_aliases) LIMIT 1`;
    if (c) return { slug: c.slug, clientId: c.id };
  }

  // 2. Wildcard subdomain? Leftmost label = slug
  const subMatch = host.match(/^([a-z0-9-]+)\.exsol\.app$/);
  if (subMatch) return resolveBySlug(subMatch[1]);

  // 3. Fallback: path-based — /menu/:slug, /book/:slug/:svc, /c/:slug/...
  const seg = url.pathname.split('/').filter(Boolean);
  const pathSlug = seg[0] === 'c' || seg[0] === 'menu' || seg[0] === 'book' ? seg[1] : null;
  if (pathSlug) return resolveBySlug(pathSlug);

  throw new NotFound();
}

Trade-offs

PatternSetup costBrandingSEOWhen to recommend
A · Path None Low (Exsol brand visible) Low (shared domain authority) Trial, signup, first-touch
B · Subdomain One-time platform: wildcard cert + DNS Mid (workspace name visible) Mid (subdomain inherits some authority) Default for paid plans

Operator setup — Pattern C (custom domain)

The end-to-end flow for a client (e.g. papa's saloon) to point papas-saloon.com at their Exsol workspace:

  1. Client side: add a CNAME record at their DNS provider: papas-saloon.com → exsoldatacollectionapp.netlify.app
  2. Exsol side: add the domain to the Netlify site's domain aliases (one-line API call or UI click)
  3. Netlify: auto-issues a Let's Encrypt cert (~minutes)
  4. Workspace: add papas-saloon.com to clients.domain_aliases TEXT[] on the matching client row (planned schema change for v2 storefront)
  5. Verify: hit https://papas-saloon.com/menu — resolver sees the host, looks up the alias, returns workspace identity, normal request flow proceeds
Why this works without code surgery
The whole codebase already scopes every query by client_id. Adding host-based resolution is purely additive: a new helper that picks the same client_id we'd have got from the path, then passes it to the existing perms/data layer unchanged. No refactor of any handler is needed.
Edge case — cross-workspace assets
Product images stored in Netlify Blobs are addressed by product-images/<client_id>/<product_id>/<blob_id>. If a workspace embeds a customer URL that points to another workspace's product (e.g. via a misconfigured affiliate link), the resolver picks the host workspace but the blob path still encodes the original workspace's client_id — so the image fetch will 404 unless explicitly cross-shared. This is the right default.

2.1 Auth Flow §auth-flow

Every protected endpoint goes through the same chain. The chain has two entry points (admin or bucket-user) and a clean L1-Owner fast-path that's identical across modules. 

// netlify/functions/_shared/permissions.ts (simplified)
export async function requirePermission(req: Request, key: string): Promise<Session> {
  // 1. Try admin session first
  try { return { kind: 'admin', ...await requireAdmin(req) }; }
  catch (e) { if (!(e instanceof UnauthorizedError)) throw e; }

  // 2. Fall through to bucket-user via JWT cookie
  const claims = await verifyBucketUserSession(readBuCookieToken(req));
  const { level_number, client_id } = await sql`SELECT ... FROM user_nodes WHERE id = ${claims.sub}`;

  // 3. L1 (Primary Owner) bypasses the matrix check
  if (level_number === 1) return { kind: 'bucket_user', level_number: 1, client_id, ... };

  // 4. Otherwise, consult the per-level permission matrix
  const matrix = await getLevelMatrix(client_id, level_number);
  if (!matrix[key]) throw new ForbiddenError(key);
  return { kind: 'bucket_user', level_number, client_id, ... };
}
POS-specific authz
POS uses a parallel helper requirePos(req, requiredKeys[]) that adds two extras: a 412 products_module_required / pos_module_not_enabled dependency gate BEFORE the perm check, and a full perms set returned in the context (so the FSM action buttons can render per-action). The L1 fast-path is identical.

2.2 Permission Model §perms

Two distinct permission models coexist in one matrix table (client_levels.permissions JSONB). Choosing the right model for a feature depends on whether the operations are CRUD on a resource (bucket/verb) or state-machine transitions / API actions (action-namespace).

Bucket / Verb model

For typical CRUD modules. Permissions are <module>.<bucket>.<verb>.

"users.users.create": true,
"users.users.edit":   true,
"users.users.delete": false,
"products.products.create": true,
Used by Files Products Team Booking Inventory Finance Procurement Warehouse Email CRM Manufacturing Workforce Data Collection Portfolio

Action-namespace model

For state-machine ops where "verbs on a resource" doesn't carve cleanly. Permissions are <module>.<action-path>.

"pos.menu.view":     true,
"pos.sale.create":   true,
"pos.sale.markPaid": true,
"pos.sale.refund":   false,
"pos.history.viewAll": false,
Used by POS (legacy-frozen)
L1 Owner = all-on by default
Both models honor level_number === 1 as an implicit grant of every key, enforced at the helper layer (requirePermission, requirePos) and mirrored in the FE gates (Sidebar, PosRouteMounts). L2+ levels require explicit grants via the Access Level admin dashboard, which now renders both models (matrix grid for bucket/verb, per-action toggles for action-namespace).

Key design decisions
  • Snapshot prices in sale_lines — later product edits don't rewrite history.
  • Per-bucket order_no — sequential within a workspace, not global; allocated via MAX+1 with 5-retry on 23505.
  • payment_ref dual-use — idempotency cache (idem:<key> prefix) + future Razorpay payment_id (no prefix). Distinguishable at read time.
  • user_nodes tree — self-referencing parent_id; subtree scoping enforced at the helper layer.
  • Append-only stock_movements ledger (053) — stock truth is the sum of typed movements (sale · purchase · adjustment · production · transfer); inventory_stock.qty_on_hand is the cached rollup, updated in the same transaction as each movement.
  • Expenses-only finance_expenses (054) — revenue is never copied; the P&L reads sales and bookings live, so Finance can't drift from the order books. DATE columns are returned as to_char(…, 'YYYY-MM-DD') to dodge the Neon driver's local-midnight day-shift.
  • CRM customers are a read-model (055) — crm_customers rows are deduped projections of sales + bookings (unique on (client_id, dedupe_key)), refreshed on demand — deliberately NOT shared identity with user_nodes, because customers carry history, not authorization.

2.4 POS Sale State Machine §fsm

Sale states are enforced server-side. Each transition checks both the FSM legality (current → target valid?) AND the caller's permission for the target action. The instore channel collapses paid→fulfilled in one call (auto-fulfill, recorded as detail.auto = true in audit).

Each transition requires the caller to hold the corresponding pos.sale.<action> permission AND the FSM source state to be valid.

Inventory hook on fulfil
When a workspace has inventory_tracking_enabled, the fulfil transition also decrements inventory_stock per sale line and appends a type='sale' row to the stock_movements ledger — fail-open (wrapped in try/catch that only logs), so an inventory hiccup can never block taking money. The FSM's no-re-fulfil rule guarantees the decrement fires at most once per sale.

2.5 Booking Module Flow §booking

Booking lets clients sell time on named resources (a stylist, a chair, a meeting room) with per-service rules — duration, per-weekday hours, optional payment up front. Two surfaces meet at the same backend: a public customer widget that auto-registers users by phone+email, and a staff calendar that manages the catalog and reads the booking grid. The core invariant the design protects is no double-booking: a single resource cannot be reserved for overlapping time ranges, enforced atomically by the database.

Customer journey (public widget)

The customer never directly contacts the database — every step is mediated by a public API endpoint with rate limiting + honeypot.

No-overbooking invariant

The hard constraint is enforced at the schema level using a PostgreSQL GIST exclusion constraint rather than application-side checks. This makes it atomic — concurrent requests for the same slot can't both succeed: 

-- bookings (planned schema for v1)
CREATE TABLE bookings (
  id           uuid PRIMARY KEY DEFAULT gen_random_uuid(),
  client_id    uuid NOT NULL REFERENCES clients(id),
  resource_id  uuid NOT NULL REFERENCES resources(id),
  service_id   uuid NOT NULL REFERENCES services(id),
  customer_user_node_id uuid REFERENCES user_nodes(id),  -- nullable for guest holds
  time_range   tstzrange NOT NULL,
  status       booking_status NOT NULL DEFAULT 'pending_payment',
  ...,
  -- atomicity: two bookings on the same resource cannot overlap
  EXCLUDE USING GIST (
    resource_id WITH =,
    time_range  WITH &&
  ) WHERE (status IN ('pending_payment', 'confirmed'))
);
Why GIST + WHERE clause
Cancelled or refunded bookings shouldn't block the slot — the partial index condition lets only active bookings hold the slot. A retry after a webhook timeout (still pending_payment) is still safe: the conflicting row IS the original, and the idempotency key on (client, phone, time_range) catches the re-submission.

Per-service payment policy

Each service declares a payment mode that drives the FSM at booking time. Resource-time holds are kept short for free services and revert to free-for-grabs if payment doesn't complete in the configured window.

Service modeInitial booking statusSlot held untilBecomes
payment: 'none' confirmed start time completed at end_time or staff marks no-show
payment: 'optional' confirmed start time same as 'none'; payment can be taken at the counter
payment: 'required' pending_payment +10 min OR webhook confirmed on Razorpay webhook · or cancelled on timeout

Staff side (calendar surface)

The owner / staff calendar is a workspace-portal page (under /c/<slug>/booking) gated on the standard bucket/verb permission model. Three primary views:

  • Day grid — vertical time axis × horizontal resources. Color-coded by status. Click a cell to view/edit a booking.
  • Service catalog — CRUD for services, duration, payment policy, per-weekday hours.
  • Resource manager — CRUD for named resources, optionally tied to a staff user_node for "the stylist who's logged in only sees their column".
Timezone handling
All time_range values are stored as tstzrange (timestamp WITH time zone). Conversion to the workspace's display timezone happens at the FE / formatter layer only — storage and the GIST overlap check are always in UTC, so a daylight-saving boundary can't accidentally create or hide an overlap.

2.6 API Patterns §api-patterns

Every function file follows the same skeleton. Consistency at this layer makes the 138 endpoints feel like one codebase.

// netlify/functions/<name>.ts
import type { Context } from '@netlify/functions';
import { jsonError, jsonOk } from './_shared/http';
import { authenticateForPermission } from './_shared/permissions';
import { logAudit } from './_shared/audit';
import { db } from './_shared/db';

export const config = { path: '/api/foo', method: 'POST' };

export default async (req: Request, _ctx: Context) => {
  if (req.method !== 'POST') return jsonError(405, 'method_not_allowed');

  // 1. Auth + perm gate (returns Response on failure)
  const auth = await authenticateForPermission(req, 'foo.bar.create');
  if (auth instanceof Response) return auth;

  // 2. Resolve tenant scope
  const scope = resolveClientIdOrRespond(auth, req);
  if (scope instanceof Response) return scope;

  // 3. Validate body with zod
  const body = FooCreateBody.safeParse(await req.json());
  if (!body.success) return jsonError(400, 'invalid_body');

  // 4. Do the work
  const sql = db();
  const [row] = await sql`INSERT INTO foos (...) VALUES (...) RETURNING *`;

  // 5. Audit
  await logAudit(auth, 'foo.created', { target_id: row.id, detail: { ... } });

  return jsonOk(row);
};

2.7 Audit & Migrations §audit

Audit trail

Every mutation writes a row to audit_log via logAudit(session, op, { target, detail }). The detail JSONB is opt-in: include enough to reconstruct intent (e.g., { from, to, auto, reason } on FSM transitions; { byte_count, table_counts } on exports).

Indexed by (client_id, occurred_at) for cheap timeline queries per workspace.

Migration system

Sequential numbered files in db/migrations/<NNN>_<name>.sql. Tracked in schema_migrations(version, applied_at). Apply with npm run migrate (dev) or DATABASE_URL=$PROD_DB_URL npm run migrate (prod).

Dry-run: npm run migrate -- --status. Idempotent on re-run via the version table — applied migrations are skipped.

Reading the numbering — why the sequence jumps 062 → 107
Width-phase migrations were allocated one number at a time by the human coordinator (001–062; 051 stays reserved for the held Payments module). For the depth phase the allocation switched to reserved per-module ranges: the block 063–139 is pre-assigned in bands, one band per module, so parallel depth sessions can never collide on a number. 107_timesheet_entries.sql — the first depth slice — is drawn from the Project-Manager/Workforce band (its own header notes "depth range 107–111"). A reviewer diffing the folder should read 063–106 as allocated-but-not-yet-claimed range, not as lost files — no migration in that span has ever existed or been applied.
Order of operations
Additive migrations: apply to prod BEFORE pushing code that depends on the new column/table.
Destructive migrations: push code FIRST (removing references), THEN apply the destructive migration. Order is inverted.

The enforcement chain (per request)

  • Enable-gate before permission check — _<module>-authz.ts returns 412 if the product is off for the workspace, then applies the level_number === 1 Owner bypass, then the matrix lookup (iron-rule order).
  • Cross-tenant guard — resolveClientId: a bucket-user request whose ?client= mismatches the JWT's pinned client is a 403 forbidden_cross_client; authorizeSubtreeScope further restricts L2+ to their own user_nodes subtree (recursive CTE).
  • Append-only audit — every mutating endpoint ends with logAudit(); audit_log has one writer (a single INSERT helper), no UPDATE/DELETE in any handler.
  • Zod at both boundaries — input bodies are schema-validated at handlers; the environment itself is Zod-validated and fails fast: a missing DATABASE_URL or a JWT_SIGNING_SECRET shorter than 32 chars refuses to boot. There is deliberately no dev fallback for the signing secret; softer secrets degrade gracefully (no RESEND_API_KEY → mail recorded as logged, never sent).
  • Webhook signature verification (booking payments) — booking-razorpay-webhook.ts rejects unsigned payloads via HMAC-SHA256 with timingSafeEqual (_booking-razorpay.ts).
  • Human-only releases — a committed .claude/hooks/block-git-push.py PreToolUse hook blocks any agent-issued git push (exit 2); deploys happen only through the human coordinator.
Deliberate decision — application-layer tenancy, no Postgres RLS
There is no Row-Level Security anywhere in the schema (verified: zero CREATE POLICY / ROW LEVEL SECURITY statements). Tenancy lives in the auth-helper layer: the JWT pins the tenant, resolveClientId checks it, and every query is parameterized by client_id. The trade-off is real and accepted: RLS would add a database-level backstop against a forgotten WHERE client_id =, at the cost of per-connection role plumbing that fits poorly with a serverless HTTP driver and one shared connection. The compensating controls are uniformity (every endpoint composes the same helpers — an endpoint that looks different is an endpoint to review) and the conformance audit. If the platform ever moves to a Postgres host where RLS is idiomatic (e.g. Supabase — see Part 3), this decision should be revisited.

Options if a client demands "an app"
PWA (manifest + service worker) is purely additive — installable icon, offline shell, push notifications on Android — with no change to the React code; it is the planned first step. Capacitor wraps the same SPA into app-store binaries if a client contract requires store presence; the cost is store review overhead, and (per Apple policy) shipping sign-in in an iOS app would require adding Apple Sign-In (Part 3, Auth). Native rewrites are not on any roadmap.
Part 3

Portability & Alternatives — the seam inventory as exit strategy

Every external dependency is reached through one owned seam file. That discipline, kept for engineering reasons, has a commercial consequence: the seam inventory doubles as a priced migration cost-sheet. When a client's profile demands a different database, auth provider, host, or payment rail, the answer is a known file and a known cost class — not a rewrite.

Principle — we don't promise every stack; we promise a priced exit from every dependency
A dependency accessed from one file costs what that file costs to rewrite. A dependency smeared across 138 handlers costs a migration project. ExSol's bet is the first shape, verified below seam-by-seam.

Database — the seam and the three exits

_shared/db.ts is the only file in the request path that constructs a database client (@neondatabase/serverless, HTTP driver, memoized singleton); everything else receives a sql tag function. There is no ORM to fight — queries are plain parameterized SQL.

  • Another Postgres host — hours. Swap the connection string and, if leaving Neon's HTTP driver, swap neon(url) for pg/pooler inside the one file. Supabase deserves a specific note: it wins for clients who want bundled BaaS — RLS, realtime, storage, and auth in one console — and adopting Supabase-Postgres does not require adopting its auth or storage; ExSol's owned layers keep working against a vanilla Postgres URL. (Only the CLI scripts in scripts/, which deliberately build their own client, also need the URL.)
  • MySQL/MariaDB — weeks, honestly. The code uses Postgres as Postgres. Real blockers found in the schema and queries: the GiST exclusion constraint + TSTZRANGE that atomically prevents double-booking (048; MySQL has no equivalent — it becomes application-level locking, a correctness regression); 10 JSONB columns across 8 tables including the permission matrix; native ENUM types; to_char formatting in 14 query files; partial indexes; RETURNING in 58 files and ON CONFLICT upserts in 12. Quote it as a dialect-migration project, not a driver swap.
  • Document DBs — a considered rejection, not a gap. The schema is deeply relational on purpose: 64 foreign-key references across 26 migrations, append-only ledgers (stock_movements, audit_log, email_outbox), a self-referencing org tree, and cross-module invariants enforced by the database (CHECKs, exclusion constraints, transactions). Reproducing those guarantees in Mongo means rebuilding the data layer and its invariants by hand — a rewrite sold as a migration. We decline it as a product decision.

Auth — add providers cheaply, or outsource the layer knowingly

All JWT mint/verify lives in _shared/session.ts; Google verification is isolated in google-verifier.ts (the only importer of google-auth-library); argon2 hashing in argon.ts. That shape makes providers additive:

  • Apple Sign-In — days. A sibling apple-verifier.ts validating Apple's ID token, same interface, same session mint. Needed for iOS-heavy client bases — and mandatory the day any native iOS wrapper ships third-party sign-in (Apple policy).
  • Microsoft / enterprise SSO — days. Same verifier pattern against Entra ID; the session layer doesn't change.
  • Auth0 / Clerk / Supabase Auth — a trade, not an upgrade. The owned layer is ~200 lines, auditable, and free at any scale. Outsourcing buys SSO federation, MFA, and compliance checkboxes fast — the right call when a client demands SOC2-style SSO on a deadline — at the price of per-MAU fees and a third party inside the auth path. The seam makes either answer available per deployment.

Hosting & functions — the biggest portability asset

Every one of the 138 functions is a web-standard (Request) ⇒ Response handler — the same signature Cloudflare Workers, Vercel Edge, and Deno Deploy execute. The @netlify/functions import is type-only. What is actually Netlify-specific: discovery (flat files), routing (config.path/method exports + the /api/* redirect), and Blobs. Porting is therefore a router shim — a table mapping paths to the existing handlers — plus the storage swap below: days to a week, with handler bodies untouched.

Storage, email, payments — proven seams

  • Storage — days. Six files call getStore (four storage wrappers + two rate-limiters); no handler imports @netlify/blobs directly, and every Blobs-touching test already mocks the seam — the swap surface is proven weekly by the suite. S3/R2/Supabase Storage slot in behind the same wrapper signatures; the rate-limit counters move to Postgres or the host's KV.
  • Email — hours. sendMail() writes the email_outbox row first and delivers through resend.ts, a raw-fetch adapter with no SDK. SES/Postmark/SMTP = one new adapter file; modules never learn the provider changed, and the outbox keeps the delivery record either way.
  • Payments — designed neutral before any rail ships. The payment_ref seam on sales, the FSM's pending_payment states, and the HMAC-verified webhook pattern are provider-agnostic; Razorpay is simply the India-first rail (spec held on 051). Stripe is the same shape for non-India clients — the eventual model is per-client rail selection, two adapter files behind one seam.

Frontend — what would not port cheaply

The SPA locks us to React and nothing else — no meta-framework, no proprietary data layer; Vite is replaceable build tooling. The honest limit is SSR/SEO: the storefront, catalog, and brand sites render client-side, which is fine for link-in-bio and direct traffic but weak for organic search. If a client's business depends on storefront SEO, the public surfaces would move to Next or Remix — weeks of work, confined to the public pages (the internal app has no SEO need and stays a SPA). That is a known, accepted trade-off, priced in advance like everything else in this chapter.
