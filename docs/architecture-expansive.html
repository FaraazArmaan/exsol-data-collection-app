ExSol Suite — Architecture, in depth

A long-form, plain-language walkthrough of what we're building and how it fits together — from the seven separate products that ship from this codebase, down through the 13 ERP modules at its heart, the way one workspace becomes seven views of itself, and the operational machinery that keeps it all running. Written so a careful reader without a software-engineering background can follow.

Chapter 1

What is ExSol?

The simplest possible description, then a more honest one.

First, in everyday terms

Imagine you run a small business — a salon, a café, a clothing store. To operate it well you need a calendar (who's booked when), a register (taking payment), a product list (what you sell, for how much), a way for customers to find you online, a way for them to book or order, a place to keep your documents, a record of who works for you, and a back office where you can see how the business is doing.

ExSol gives you all of that in one piece of software, but adapted to your business. We don't make one app for salons and a different one for cafés. We make one platform that's reconfigurable: each business turns on the parts they need, brands them, and uses them.

Now, the technical framing

ExSol is a multi-tenant SaaS suite. There is one codebase, one running platform, and one database — but inside it, every business (we call them tenants or clients) gets a private workspace that no other business can see. From the same codebase the system produces seven distinct customer-facing products: an internal ERP for the operators, a data-collection onboarding flow, a booking system (customer + staff), an ecommerce storefront, an in-store POS, a catalog showcase site, and per-brand portfolio sites.

The phrase "data-collection app" in this repo's name reflects history, not present scope. The system started as a way to collect product data during client onboarding. It has since grown into the platform described here — a full operational suite. The repo name is a historical artifact; the architecture is what's described below.

The Seven Products

Seven distinct things ExSol ships to its users — each with its own audience, URL, and reason to exist. They share a single backend.

The Internal App (ERP)

The operator's home base. Where the business runs itself.
Used by Owners and staff of every workspace to manage their entire business: catalog, inventory, orders, customers, finance, HR, marketing, and 7 more modules. Lives at /c/<slug>/... in the browser. Today: 5 modules live (POS, Booking, File Manager, Product Manager, Workspace/AMS); 8 more modules planned. This is also where every other product gets configured — toggling a storefront, adding products to the catalog, setting business hours, granting staff permissions.

A focused mini-app that gets a new client onboarded fast.

The Booking System (staff + customer)

A double-sided appointment system. One for the business, one for the customer.
Two surfaces of the same data: a staff calendar at /c/<slug>/booking for the business owner / staff to see their day and manage services, resources, and hours; plus an anonymous customer widget at /c/<slug>/book where a guest can pick a service, a time, and a stylist, enter their name + phone, and reserve a slot — no account required. The customer's online ordering page for any business that opts in.

The In-Store POS System

A cashier register, browser-based, tap-friendly.

For in-store sales, paid → fulfilled in one click (auto-fulfill); for pickup channel, two clicks (mark paid when payment received, mark fulfilled when customer picks up). Same backend as the ecommerce storefront — different UI, different default channel.
live

The Catalog Website

Like the storefront, but no purchase. Just a beautiful showcase.

Reuses every component of the storefront except the cart and checkout.
planned

A bespoke single-brand site, assembled from suite components.

Each one is assembled per-client from a kit of components (the same hero, gallery, calendar, contact form, product grid, etc. used across the other products) wired up to that client's data. Custom domain, custom palette, custom layout. The reason to build this in-platform rather than letting clients hire a separate web agency is that the brand site stays in sync with the Internal App — change a product's price in the catalog and it updates on the brand site automatically.

Chapter 3

The 13 ERP Modules

The Internal App is structured around 13 ERP modules — the same set you'd find in any traditional enterprise resource planning system. Each is a self-contained area of business operations with its own data, permissions, UI, and backend.

What is "ERP," anyway?

ERP stands for Enterprise Resource Planning. The idea is old and simple: every business has a fixed set of repeating concerns — money in, money out, who works here, what we sell, what we have in the warehouse, who our customers are, what's being made. An ERP is the single software where all of those concerns are tracked, so the answer to "are we doing well?" doesn't require stitching together five spreadsheets.

The version of "ERP" you might know from corporate IT (SAP, Oracle, Microsoft Dynamics) is gigantic, expensive, and aimed at multinationals. ExSol is the same conceptual idea — same 13 modules — built for businesses with 1 to 100 employees, who actually use the apps from their phone, and who pay monthly. That's the gap we're filling.

The books. Money in, money out, P&L, accounts receivable / payable, payment ledgers, basic tax filing inputs.

planned

Making things. BOMs (bills of material: what goes into a finished product), work orders, production runs.

planned

4. Inventory Management

What we have. Stock levels per warehouse, internal transfers, reorder points so we don't run out.

planned

What customers asked for. Order intake from every channel (in-store, online, phone), fulfillment, returns. Today: the POS sales table is the partial implementation; live for the two POS channels.

Chapter 4 · Foundations

One codebase, many products

How do seven distinct products come from one Git repo?

The analogy

Think of a TV studio that records one news program and then re-edits the same footage into seven different formats — a one-hour broadcast, a 90-second social clip, a podcast cut, a long-form YouTube version, a written transcript, an alert text. The source is the same; the packaging is different. ExSol is a similar idea, but with software components instead of footage.

In code, this means three things

1 · A shared component library

The same React component that draws a "product tile" with a photo and a price appears on the POS, on the ecommerce storefront, on the catalog site, and in the Product Manager's edit page. It lives once in src/modules/products/components/ProductTile.tsx and is imported by every page that needs it. When we improve the tile (better photo cropping, smoother hover state) every product improves.

2 · A shared data layer

The PostgreSQL database has one products table per workspace. The POS reads it, the storefront reads it, the catalog reads it. There's no copy-paste between products, no synchronization lag, no data inconsistency. The downside is that one bad write to products shows up in every surface immediately — which is why audit trails and the permission model exist.

3 · Per-route gates

The URL is the configuration. /c/<slug>/pos/menu says: render the staff POS view. /menu/<slug> says: render the public storefront. Both URLs end up fetching from the same products endpoint, but the route gate decides which auth model applies (staff JWT vs. anonymous) and which UI to render.

The challenge is keeping the "shared library" abstraction honest: when a sibling chat builds a new product, they have to resist the temptation to copy-paste an existing component and "just tweak it a little." The discipline is to extract the difference as a prop, not fork the component. Today's session preserved this for the most part — the SideCartPanel's "merge-safe" refactor is exactly that: its qty controls became optional props so the same panel works for the staff POS (with steppers) and the guest storefront (without).

Chapter 5 · Foundations

The Tenant Model

How one platform serves many businesses without leaking data between them.

First, an analogy

Think of a large coworking building. There are 200 companies inside, each in their own room. They all share the lobby, the elevators, the wifi, the cleaners. But you can't walk into another company's room and read their mail. The building's security model is: each room has a key, and your key only opens your room.

ExSol is the building. Each business — we call them a client or tenant or workspace (these all mean the same thing) — is one room. The data they create stays in their room. The other 199 businesses on the platform can never read it, even if they go poking at the URL.

In code

Every meaningful data row in the database has a client_id column. The application code, at every entry point, scopes every query by client_id. There is no Postgres Row-Level Security — tenancy is enforced at the application layer through three coordinated mechanisms:

  1. JWT cookie carries the tenant binding. When a staff member logs into a workspace, the cookie they receive contains their user_node_id AND their client_id. They cannot "log into" a different workspace without obtaining a different cookie. The cookie is signed; tampering would invalidate it.
  2. Every endpoint checks the URL's client param against the cookie. If a malicious user hand-edits a URL from /c/papa-s-saloon/pos/menu to /c/another-business/pos/menu and tries to load it, the backend sees: cookie says client=papa-s-saloon, URL says client=another-business, these don't match → return 403 forbidden_cross_client.
  3. Every SQL query is parameterized by client_id. The auth helper resolves the client_id from the cookie, hands it to the handler, and the handler uses it in every SQL query. There is no query that pulls data without a client_id filter.
What about admins?

Admins (the operators of ExSol itself — Faraaz and team) have a different cookie that grants cross-workspace access. Their authentication is separate (the admin login flow, the admin dashboard at /). When an admin acts on a specific workspace, they pass ?client=<uuid> explicitly in the URL — which is how the backend knows which workspace to target.

Bucket-users (workspace owners and staff) cannot pass ?client= — it's silently ignored or rejected if it mismatches their JWT. This asymmetry is the cross-client guard.

Chapter 6 · Foundations

Three tiers of users

Who logs in, what they can see, how the system tells them apart.

In everyday terms

Three kinds of people use ExSol. The platform operators (us) — we run the building. The workspace owners and staff — they're the tenants who live in their room. And the customers of those tenants — they're guests who walk into the storefront, place an order or book an appointment, and leave. Each group has a different door, a different key, different permissions.

The three sessions
TierWhoAuth mechanismScope
Admin ExSol team (us) Username + password, lives in admins table; argon2 hashed Cross-workspace; can act on any tenant by passing ?client=
Bucket-user Workspace Owners + their staff Username + password OR Google OAuth, lives in user_node_credentials; JWT cookie scoped to one client One workspace only; subtree-scoped if they're L2+
Guest End-customers using a storefront or booking widget No login. Identified by IP rate-limiting + honeypot + manage-token cookie for receipt links Only the public endpoints (storefront menu, booking availability, create + manage their own order/booking)
Levels within bucket-users

Within a workspace, staff are organized into levels: L1 (Primary / Owner) at the top, then L2, L3, etc. The Owner can see and do everything in their workspace by default. L2+ staff need explicit permissions granted via the access-levels admin UI. This level hierarchy is what makes "Stylist A can only see her own bookings, Owner can see everyone's" work cleanly.

Chapter 7 · Foundations

The Permission Model

How the system decides whether an action is allowed.

The everyday version

Imagine the workspace as an office with several rooms — products room, bookings room, files room. The Owner has master keys to every room. Each staff member gets a key ring with only the keys they need: maybe "products: can look at, can edit" but not "files: can delete." The set of keys is called their permission matrix, and it's stored once per level (L2's keys, L3's keys, etc.).

The technical model

Permissions are strings like products.products.view (module = products, bucket = products, verb = view). Each level in a workspace has a JSONB map of { "permissions.key": true } in the client_levels table. The auth chain looks up the level for the requesting user, consults this matrix, and either approves the action or returns 403.

Model A: Bucket × Verb

For CRUD-style operations (Create / Read / Update / Delete on a resource). Keys look like <module>.<bucket>.<verb>. 

products.products.create
products.categories.edit
booking.customers.view
booking.employees.edit
files.documents.delete
Used by ProductsBookingFilesTeam

Model B: Action namespace

For state-machine operations where "verbs on a resource" doesn't carve cleanly. Keys look like <module>.<action>. 

pos.menu.view
pos.sale.markPaid
pos.sale.refund
pos.history.viewAll
Used by POS
L1 Owner is all-on by default

Both models honor a special rule: if level_number === 1, skip the matrix check and grant every permission. This is what makes a fresh workspace usable on day one without configuring keys. The Owner can immediately log in and do everything. L2+ staff need explicit grants.

This sounds simple, but it took shipping the same fix to three different modules (POS, Booking, and the public storefront helpers) before it became a documented pattern. See the operational traps section below for why.

Chapter 8 · How a request works

End-to-end request lifecycle

What happens between a click in the browser and a row in the database.

Walking through it slowly

Let's follow a single click: a staff member at papa-s-saloon clicks "Mark paid" on order S-00002. Here's everything that happens, in order.

1
The browser sends a network request. A POST to /api/pos/sales/<id>/state with body { action: 'markPaid', paymentMethod: 'cash' } and the user's session cookie attached.
2
Netlify Edge receives it. Netlify is the platform we host on — it has servers all over the world. The request hits whichever edge node is closest to the user. The edge consults its function registry and figures out: this URL routes to the function file at netlify/functions/pos-sale-state.ts.
3
The function spins up. Netlify Functions are short-lived processes — they start fresh per request (or per small batch), run their code, return a response, and shut down. This is "serverless." The function file we wrote runs.
4
Auth chain runs. The function calls requirePos(req, ['pos.sale.markPaid']). That helper: reads the cookie, validates the JWT signature, looks up the user_node in the database, checks that POS is enabled for this workspace, checks the user holds the markPaid permission (skipped if they're L1 Owner), and either returns an authorized context or returns a 403/401 response.
5
The handler does the work. Validates the body with zod (rejects malformed input with 400), consults the FSM (is the current sale state pending_payment? if not, return 409), updates the database row inside a transaction, and — because the channel is "instore" — auto-advances the state from paid to fulfilled in the same call.
6
Audit log entry is written. A row is inserted into audit_log with op: 'pos.sale.markPaid', the actor's user_node_id, the target sale's id, and a JSON blob with the from/to states. This is the immutable history of what happened.
7
Response goes back. The handler returns jsonOk({ sale: updatedRow }). Netlify Edge forwards it to the browser. The browser's React code receives the JSON, updates the page (in theory — there's an open UX bug where it doesn't auto-refetch, see operational cliffs).
Total time: ~200-400ms

A well-behaved POS state transition runs end-to-end in about a quarter to half a second. Most of that is network latency, not computation. The actual database write is <5ms; the function cold-start (first request after idle) adds 100-200ms; subsequent requests in the same minute are warm.

Chapter 9 · How a request works

The Data Model

The shape of the database, and why it's shaped that way.

A short orientation

The database is one PostgreSQL instance with about 40 tables. Every table either belongs to a workspace (via client_id) or is platform-level (the admins table is the only one). Conceptually, the tables form a tree: clients is the root, and most other tables hang off it.

The core tables, by group

Identity

  • admins — ExSol team members. Cross-workspace.
  • clients — workspaces. One row per business. Has a slug (URL identifier) and a name.
  • user_nodes — every person inside a workspace: Owner, staff, customers. Has a level_number, a parent_id (for L2 reporting to L1), and a role_id.
  • user_node_credentials — login credentials for any user_node that needs them (Owner, staff). Customers don't have credentials unless they sign up.
  • client_levels — the permission matrix per level per workspace. Stored as JSONB.
  • client_roles — named role labels (Owner, Cashier, Stylist) with optional color and bucket_family.

Configuration

  • client_enabled_products — which modules each workspace has turned on. A workspace without "pos" enabled here cannot use the POS module even if their code path tries.
  • client_cardinality_rules — limits like "max 1 user per L1" — used by AMS for tier policies.

Products (catalog)

  • products — the catalog. Has name, type, price_cents, optional sale_price_cents, discount_percent, status (active / draft / archived), pos_visible, storefront_visible, and a long tail of Phase B columns (sku, gtin, category, brand, etc.). 49 columns total.
  • product_categories — categorization tree.
  • product_images — image files attached to products, stored in Netlify Blobs with thumb pipeline.

Sales (orders)

  • sales — order header. order_no (per-bucket sequence), status (FSM enum), channel (instore / pickup / online), source (storefront / instore / pickup / online), customer info (plain text, no FK to a customer table), payment_ref (idempotency key + future Razorpay id), payment_method, paid_at, fulfilled_at, cancelled_at, refunded_at.
  • sale_lines — one row per line in the cart, with snapshot prices so future product edits don't rewrite history.

Bookings

  • booking_settings — per-workspace timezone, weekly schedule, slot interval, cancellation cutoff.
  • booking_resources — named resources (Stylist A, Chair 2). Active flag.
  • booking_resource_time_off — vacation/blocks per resource.
  • booking_services — services offered (Haircut, Color), with duration, price, payment_mode (pay_at_venue / deposit / full_upfront).
  • bookings — the actual appointment. resource_id, service_id, customer_user_node_id (nullable for blocked time), time_range (tstzrange), status enum, manage_token. Has a GIST exclusion constraint preventing two bookings overlapping on the same resource.

Files

  • files — uploaded documents (PDFs, photos, videos). Per-client storage path in Netlify Blobs.
  • file_categories — finance/HR/legal/etc. taxonomy.
  • file_audience — who can see this file (role / node / user grants).
  • workspace_storage_quotas — per-client byte limit + current usage cache.

Audit

  • audit_log — every mutation writes a row. actor_admin OR actor_user_node, op (e.g. pos.sale.markPaid), client_id, target_type, target_id, detail JSONB. Cross-cutting; not owned by any single module.
Key design decisions you should know
  • Snapshot prices on sale_lines and product_name on sale_lines. When a sale is created, we copy the product name and unit price into the line row. Future edits to the product don't change history.
  • customer_name and customer_phone as plain text on sales. No foreign key to a customer table. Why: guest sales would otherwise force every guest to create a record. Future CRM integration can match-or-link these post-hoc.
  • tstzrange + GIST exclusion on bookings. Concurrent requests for the same slot can't both succeed because the database itself enforces the no-overlap rule. The application doesn't need to "lock" anything.
  • JSONB for permission matrices. Permissions vary in shape per module; a rigid relational model would be painful. JSONB gives flexibility at the cost of slightly more complex queries.
Chapter 10 · How a request works

State machines

Why most operational records have a well-defined lifecycle and what we get for the trouble.

Why states matter

Take a POS sale. It can be pending payment, paid, fulfilled, cancelled, or refunded. Each transition between these states is a business event with its own rules: you can refund a paid sale, but you can't refund a cancelled one. You can mark fulfilled from paid, but not from pending. If we didn't encode these rules, a careless click could put a sale into a nonsense state.

A finite state machine (FSM) is the formal name for "a set of states + the rules about which transitions are legal." We use FSMs for sales (5 states), bookings (6 states), and most operational records.

The POS sale FSM
pending_payment → paid       (action: markPaid)
pending_payment → cancelled  (action: cancel)
paid → fulfilled             (action: fulfill — auto if channel='instore')
paid → refunded              (action: refund)
fulfilled → refunded         (action: refund)

// channel-aware: instore auto-fulfills, pickup requires two clicks

The FSM is enforced server-side in the handler before any database write. The client UI also reads the current state and shows only the legal action buttons (so the user can't even click "fulfill" when the sale is still pending), but the server check is the authority — a malicious client could forge the request and the server would still reject illegal transitions with 409.

The booking FSM is similar but channel-aware in a different way
Per-service payment policy decides whether a booking starts at pending_payment or confirmed. payment: 'none' → confirmed immediately. payment: 'required' → pending_payment, with a cleanup cron that cancels stale bookings after 15 min. payment: 'optional' behaves like 'none' but allows post-hoc payment at the counter.
Chapter 11 · How a request works

Audit trail

How we know what happened, who did it, and when.

Why every business needs an audit log

A customer complains: "I ordered three coffees, you charged me for five." The Owner asks: "Who took the order? When? What did they enter?" Without an audit log, there's no answer. With one: a few SQL queries and you can reconstruct the exact sequence of events.

Audit logs are also legal protection (for disputes), operational insight (was the storefront slow because someone was hammering it?), and a debugging aid (this row looks wrong — who edited it?).

The audit_log table is append-only by convention (no UPDATEs, no DELETEs in normal use). Every mutation across the platform writes a row via the logAudit(session, op, { target, detail }) helper. The detail JSONB blob is module-specific: 

// Example: storefront sale created by an anonymous guest
{
  occurred_at: '2026-06-30T14:48:18Z',
  op: 'pos.sale.created',
  actor_admin: null,
  actor_user_node: null,  // both null = anonymous guest
  client_id: 'a9c733b4-...',
  target_type: 'sale',
  target_id: '050e97fc-...',
  detail: {
    lines: 1,
    source: 'storefront',
    channel: 'pickup',
    total_cents: 1000
  }
}

What makes this powerful: the op namespace is structured (module.entity.action), so you can ask "all POS state transitions in the last 24 hours" with a simple WHERE op LIKE 'pos.sale.%'. The detail JSONB captures whatever the module thinks is interesting at the moment — for state transitions, the from/to states; for exports, the byte count + table counts; for FSM auto-transitions, a flag like auto: true so you can distinguish "staff clicked twice" from "system auto-advanced."

Anatomy of a module

What a "module" actually consists of in this codebase, layer by layer.

When a sibling chat builds a new module, they're writing code in roughly seven places. The example below is the POS module — but every module follows this same structure.

Chapter 13 · How modules are built

The Registry

How modules announce themselves to the rest of the system.

The Registry is a static configuration in src/modules/registry/ that knows about every Module and every Product. It's the source of truth for:

  • What modules exist and their labels (e.g. "Booking & Calendar" not just "booking")
  • What permission keys each module has (e.g. pos.sale.markPaid)
  • Which products unlock which modules (e.g. enabling the 'saloon-booking' product unlocks the booking module)
  • What "buckets" each module exposes (used by the bucket×verb permission matrix UI)

The registry is consumed by: /api/u-me (to figure out what enabled_modules to return for the current user), AccessLevelDashboard (to render the permissions matrix), and the sidebar (to show which links to render). 

// src/modules/registry/modules.ts
export const moduleRegistry = {
  booking: bookingManifest,
  payments: paymentsManifest,
  products: productsManifest,
  pos: posManifest,
} as const;

// src/modules/registry/products.ts
export const productRegistry = {
  'saloon-booking': saloonBookingProduct,
  'products':       productsProduct,
  'pos':            posProduct,    // requires: ['products']
} as const;

The "products require products" wording is a quirk: posProduct.requires = ['products'] means "the POS product requires the Products product to also be enabled for the workspace." The POS reads the catalog from the Products module, so enabling POS without Products would leave the POS with nothing to sell.

Chapter 14 · How modules are built

API patterns

What every backend function looks like, structurally.

Every Netlify Function in this repo follows the same skeleton. Once you can read one, you can read all ~91 of them. The shape is: 

// netlify/functions/<name>.ts
import type { Context } from '@netlify/functions';
import { jsonError, jsonOk } from './_shared/http';
import { requirePos } from './_pos-authz';
import { logAudit } from './_shared/audit';
import { db } from './_shared/db';
import { SaleStateBody } from './_pos-validators';

export const config = { path: '/api/pos/sales/:id/state', method: 'POST' };

export default async (req: Request, _ctx: Context) => {
  // 1. Auth + perm gate (returns Response on failure)
  const auth = await requirePos(req, ['pos.sale.markPaid']);
  if (!auth.ok) return auth.res;

  // 2. Validate body with zod
  const body = SaleStateBody.safeParse(await req.json());
  if (!body.success) return jsonError(400, 'invalid_body', { details: body.error });

  // 3. Do the work (FSM transition, channel-aware auto-fulfill)
  const sql = db();
  const [updated] = await sql`UPDATE sales SET status = ..., paid_at = now() WHERE ...`;

  // 4. Audit
  await logAudit(auth.ctx, 'pos.sale.markPaid', {
    target_id: updated.id,
    detail: { from: 'pending_payment', to: 'paid' },
  });

  return jsonOk(updated);
};

Auth → validate → work → audit → respond. Always in that order. The auth helper is module-specific (requirePos for POS, requireBooking for booking) but they all share the same pattern: enable-gate first, then permission check, with the L1 Owner bypass after the enable-gate.

Chapter 15 · Operations

Deployment

How code goes from a developer's laptop to a customer's screen.

The non-technical version

We use Netlify, a service that takes our code from GitHub, builds the website + backend, and runs it on their servers. Every time someone with permission pushes new code to the main branch on GitHub, Netlify notices, runs the build, and replaces the live version with the new one. No manual server management; no SSH; no "did the deploy work" anxiety beyond a "ready" signal in the Netlify dashboard.

The database is hosted on a different service called Neon — they run PostgreSQL, and we have two separate "branches" (think: copies) of our database: one for development, one for production. Changing the database structure (called a "migration") is a manual step done before pushing code that depends on the change.

The full sequence for a production push
  1. Verify the prod Neon endpoint is the right one: echo $PROD_DB_URL | grep dawn-bird. If we get this wrong and run a destructive migration on the dev database thinking it's prod, we'd silently break the dev workflow.
  2. For additive migrations (new tables, new columns): run npm run migrate against prod FIRST. This is because code that expects the column would crash if the column doesn't exist yet. Once the column is there, code that doesn't yet use it is fine — additive migrations are forward-compatible.
  3. For destructive migrations (dropping columns, dropping tables): run code FIRST. This is the opposite of additive — destructive migrations are backward-incompatible. You want the old code that uses the column to stop running before you drop the column.
  4. git push origin main. Netlify auto-builds (~30-50s) and auto-deploys.
  5. Watch for the "ready" state. Probe new endpoints — if they 404 with SPA HTML, the Edge function registry hasn't picked them up yet (this is a known trap — see operational cliffs). Run netlify api restoreSiteDeploy to nudge it.
  6. If the alias-not-promoted trap fires (alias URL serves the old bundle hash while per-deploy URL serves the new one), restoreSiteDeploy again.
  7. UI smoke on a real workspace. Verify the new feature actually works for an end user, not just that the deploy went green.
Chapter 16 · Operations

Database migrations

How we evolve the database schema over time without losing data.

Migrations are numbered SQL files in db/migrations/. Each one is a small set of CREATE TABLE, ALTER TABLE, CREATE INDEX statements that describe a change. They're applied in numeric order (001, 002, …, 049). Once applied, a row is inserted into schema_migrations so the runner knows to skip it next time.

Today the repo has 49 migrations. The names are descriptive: 038_products_discount_percent.sql adds a discount percent column; 040_sales.sql creates the sales table; 046_workspace_storage_quota.sql creates the quota table.

Parallel-development friction surfaces when two sibling chats both reserve the same migration number. Today, the POS-v2 storefront work, the Booking work, and the File Manager Phase B work all branched independently and each tried to claim 043/044/045. We resolved by applying POS-v2's first, renumbering Booking's to 047/048/049, and File Manager landed cleanly at 046 (the gap we'd left). The feedback_booking_migration_number_coordination memory documents this.

Chapter 17 · Operations

Operational cliffs

Things that have gone wrong, written down so they don't go wrong again.

Every system accumulates a list of subtle failure modes that you only learn by hitting them. ExSol has a handful, all documented as memory entries and many in the architecture diagrams. The most important five:

The new-function-404 trap

Symptom: a new Netlify Function is in the deploy's function manifest, but probing its URL returns the SPA's 404 HTML instead of the function's response. Cause: Edge function routing registry hasn't caught up with the new function. Fix: netlify api restoreSiteDeploy nudges it. Always probe new endpoints after deploying them; never assume "ready" state means the routing is registered.

The alias-not-promoted trap

Symptom: the build produces a new JS / CSS bundle hash, but the workspace alias keeps serving HTML that references the previous hash. The new code is technically deployed but nobody is loading it. Detection: compare the bundle hash on the per-deploy URL vs. the alias URL. Fix: restoreSiteDeploy. Today's session hit this 4 times.

The browser-cache trap

Symptom: after a successful deploy, an open browser tab still loads the previous bundle (the HTML it loaded is cached in the browser, and that HTML references the old hash). Detection: compare document.querySelector('script[src*="/assets/"]').src in the open tab vs. curl /. Fix: hard reload. Always tell users after a deploy: refresh your tabs.

The L1-Owner bypass omission

Symptom: Owner of a workspace tries to use a newly-shipped module and gets blank UI or 403 errors, even though they "should" have access. Cause: the module's auth helper does a strict permission matrix check without the L1 bypass. Default-empty matrix means L1 Owner has no keys, so they're denied. Fix: add if (level_number === 1) return full-perm-set after the module enable-gate. Mirrors requirePos as the reference. Has now shipped 3 times (POS Phase 1, Booking, public storefront helpers) — memory entry feedback_module_l1_bypass_pattern documents the fix.

Function-shape collisions and naming

Three related ones to watch: (1) Two functions can't share the same config.path without config.method to discriminate (one will silently lose at the Edge layer). (2) Functions in subdirectories netlify/functions/foo/ are NOT auto-discovered as separate functions — the whole folder is treated as ONE function. Flat layout only: netlify/functions/foo-action.ts. (3) Integration tests bypass Netlify's routing, so a test suite can pass while routing is misconfigured.

Chapter 18 · Reference

Glossary

Terms that show up throughout this document, defined in one place.

tenant / client / workspace
Three names for the same thing: one business inside ExSol. Has a unique slug (URL identifier) and a name. All their data is scoped to their client_id.
module
A self-contained area of business functionality (POS, Booking, Files, etc.). Each module has its own database tables, permission keys, backend functions, and frontend pages.
product (in registry context)
A purchasable unit that unlocks one or more modules. Confusing word — the same English word also refers to physical things in the catalog. In the registry context, "product" = "what you buy to enable this module."
bucket
A category of data within a module that can be CRUD'd. "Customers" is a bucket of the Booking module; "Employees" is another. Permission keys take the form module.bucket.verb.
L1 / L2 / L3
Levels within a workspace. L1 is the Primary Owner; L2 is one step below (managers); L3 below that (staff). The level hierarchy is used for both permissions and reporting structure (parent_id on user_nodes).
bucket-user
A user_node that has login credentials (Owner, staff). Distinguished from admins (cross-workspace) and customers (no login).
JWT
JSON Web Token. A signed string that carries the bucket-user's identity (sub, email, client_id). Stored in a cookie called bu_session.
FSM
Finite State Machine. A formal model of "states + legal transitions." Used for sales (5 states), bookings (6 states).
GIST exclusion constraint
A PostgreSQL feature that prevents overlap on a range column. Used by bookings to atomically prevent two bookings for the same resource at the same time. Concurrent requests can't both succeed.
idempotency key
A token the client sends with a write request so the server can detect "this is a retry, not a new request." Used by sale-create and booking-create.
migration
A numbered SQL file that changes the database schema. Applied in order, recorded in schema_migrations.
Netlify Function
A short-lived serverless process that handles one HTTP request. We have ~91 of them. Each lives in a .ts file in netlify/functions/.
Neon
The PostgreSQL hosting service we use. Supports "branching" — the dev and prod databases are conceptually two branches of the same database, with the same schema but different data.
Sibling chat
One of several Claude Code chats running in parallel on different feature branches. The "main chat" (this one) owns integration + deployment. POS / Booking / Login+AMS / Product Manager / File Manager each have their own sibling chat owning their respective modules.
Handoff doc
A markdown file at docs/superpowers/handoffs/ that summarizes "where I left off" so the next agent (or next-day-me) can continue work. The convention is: sections marked OVERWRITE get rewritten each milestone; sections marked APPEND grow over time.
