ExSol Suite — Architecture, in depth

A long-form, plain-language walkthrough of what we're building and how it fits together — from the seven separate products that ship from this codebase, down through the 13 ERP modules at its heart, the way one workspace becomes seven views of itself, and the operational machinery that keeps it all running. Written so a careful reader without a software-engineering background can follow.

Chapter 1

What is ExSol?

The simplest possible description, then a more honest one.

First, in everyday terms

Imagine you run a small business — a salon, a café, a clothing store. To operate it well you need a calendar (who's booked when), a register (taking payment), a product list (what you sell, for how much), a way for customers to find you online, a way for them to book or order, a place to keep your documents, a record of who works for you, and a back office where you can see how the business is doing.

ExSol gives you all of that in one piece of software, but adapted to your business. We don't make one app for salons and a different one for cafés. We make one platform that's reconfigurable: each business turns on the parts they need, brands them, and uses them.

Now, the technical framing

ExSol is a multi-tenant SaaS suite. There is one codebase, one running platform, and one database — but inside it, every business (we call them tenants or clients) gets a private workspace that no other business can see. From the same codebase the system produces seven distinct customer-facing products: an internal ERP for the operators, a data-collection onboarding flow, a booking system (customer + staff), an ecommerce storefront, an in-store POS, a catalog showcase site, and per-brand portfolio sites.

The phrase "data-collection app" in this repo's name reflects history, not present scope. The system started as a way to collect product data during client onboarding. It has since grown into the platform described here — a full operational suite. The repo name is a historical artifact; the architecture is what's described below. (Fittingly, a literal Data Collection App has since shipped as one of the seven products — see Chapter 2.)

Chapter 2

The Seven Products

Seven distinct things ExSol ships to its users — each with its own audience, URL, and reason to exist. They share a single backend. As of this revision, all seven are live: the last three (Data Collection, Catalog, Brand Portfolio) shipped as v1s in one public-surface wave.

1

The Internal App (ERP)

The operator's home base. Where the business runs itself.
Used by Owners and staff of every workspace to manage their entire business: catalog, inventory, orders, customers, finance, HR, marketing, and more. Lives at /c/<slug>/... in the browser. Today: 18 of the 19 registered modules are live — POS, Booking, File Manager, Product Manager, Workspace/AMS, Analytics, the first ERP v1 wave (Inventory, Finance, Procurement, Warehouse, plus Email & Notifications), the second wave (CRM, Manufacturing, Workforce + Project Service), and the third wave (Supply Chain, Marketing Automation) — every row of the 13-module ERP table is now real software. The one exception: Payments stays a held registry stub. This is also where every other product gets configured — toggling a storefront, adding products to the catalog, setting business hours, granting staff permissions.
live (18 modules; all 13 ERP rows)
2

The Data Collection App

A focused mini-app that gets a new client onboarded fast.
When a new business signs up, the slowest part of onboarding is loading their product catalog. This product is a standalone, single-purpose URL for exactly that. An operator generates a single-use, expiring token (migration 061, onboard_tokens); the client opens /onboard/<token> — no login, token possession is the authorization (unknown token → 404, used or expired → 410) — and bulk-loads their products. Everything lands in the same products table as the Internal App — there is no separate data pipeline, so the rest of the workspace is preconfigured around the catalog the moment it's in.
live (v1)
3

The Booking System (staff + customer)

A double-sided appointment system. One for the business, one for the customer.
Two surfaces of the same data: a staff calendar at /c/<slug>/booking for the business owner / staff to see their day and manage services, resources, and hours; plus an anonymous customer widget at /c/<slug>/book where a guest can pick a service, a time, and a stylist, enter their name + phone, and reserve a slot — no account required. Behind both, the same bookings table, the same booking_resources, the same atomic no-overbooking constraint. Today: live, end-to-end smoke completed.
live
4

The Ecommerce Storefront

The customer's online ordering page for any business that opts in.
A public, anonymous, mobile-friendly menu+cart at /menu/<slug>. The customer browses the same products the in-store POS shows, adds to cart, enters minimal details, and places a pickup or delivery order. Pay-on-pickup by default (no Razorpay required to launch). The order flows into the same sales table as the in-store POS — but with source='storefront' and a null staff attribution, so the operator can see both channels side-by-side in the order list. Today: live, smoke verified, currently enabled on papa-s-saloon.
live
5

The In-Store POS System

A cashier register, browser-based, tap-friendly.
Lives inside the Internal App at /c/<slug>/pos/menu. Staff browse products, tap to add to a side cart, enter customer info, mark paid, and watch the order's state advance through a finite state machine. For in-store sales, paid → fulfilled in one click (auto-fulfill); for pickup channel, two clicks (mark paid when payment received, mark fulfilled when customer picks up). Same backend as the ecommerce storefront — different UI, different default channel.
live
6

The Catalog Website

Like the storefront, but no purchase. Just a beautiful showcase.
Some businesses want a customer-facing site that displays their products or services but doesn't accept orders — interior designers showing portfolios, B2B suppliers showing line cards, restaurants showing menus before opening. Live at /catalog/<slug> (migration 061): same product catalog as the ecommerce storefront, same browsing UX, served by pub-catalog, but the "Add to cart" button is replaced with a contact link. Reuses the storefront's components minus the cart and checkout — exactly the shared-library promise of Chapter 4, cashed in.
live (v1)
7

Brand Portfolio Sites

A bespoke single-brand site, assembled from suite components.
Some clients want a fully-branded website that uses the suite's data and components but doesn't look like a generic SaaS template. v1 is live (migration 062): a per-client brand_site_config row holds a sections JSONB (per-section enable flags + copy) and a published flag; the public site renders at /site/<slug> only when published, and owners assemble it in an internal editor at /c/<slug>/brand-site. The reason to build this in-platform rather than letting clients hire a separate web agency is that the brand site stays in sync with the Internal App — change a product's price in the catalog and it updates on the brand site automatically. Custom domains are the follow-up, not the v1.
live (v1)
Chapter 3

The 13 ERP Modules

The Internal App is structured around 13 ERP modules — the same set you'd find in any traditional enterprise resource planning system. Each is a self-contained area of business operations with its own data, permissions, UI, and backend.

What is "ERP," anyway?

ERP stands for Enterprise Resource Planning. The idea is old and simple: every business has a fixed set of repeating concerns — money in, money out, who works here, what we sell, what we have in the warehouse, who our customers are, what's being made. An ERP is the single software where all of those concerns are tracked, so the answer to "are we doing well?" doesn't require stitching together five spreadsheets.

The version of "ERP" you might know from corporate IT (SAP, Oracle, Microsoft Dynamics) is gigantic, expensive, and aimed at multinationals. ExSol is the same conceptual idea — same 13 modules — built for businesses with 1 to 100 employees, who actually use the apps from their phone, and who pay monthly. That's the gap we're filling.

The books. Money in, money out, P&L, accounts receivable / payable, payment ledgers, basic tax filing inputs. Today: v1 live — an expenses ledger plus a monthly P&L computed live over sales, bookings, and expenses.

Buying things. Purchase orders, supplier catalog, RFQs (requests for quotes), goods-received notes. Today: v1 live — suppliers + a purchase-order state machine whose receive step puts stock into Inventory.

live (v1)

live (v1)

They share the same auth, tenant, and permission infrastructure as the 13 ERP modules.

Chapter 4 · Foundations

One codebase, many products

How do seven distinct products come from one Git repo?

The analogy

Think of a TV studio that records one news program and then re-edits the same footage into seven different formats — a one-hour broadcast, a 90-second social clip, a podcast cut, a long-form YouTube version, a written transcript, an alert text. The source is the same; the packaging is different. ExSol is a similar idea, but with software components instead of footage.

In code, this means three things

1 · A shared component library

The same React component that draws a "product tile" with a photo and a price appears on the POS, on the ecommerce storefront, on the catalog site, and in the Product Manager's edit page. It lives once in src/modules/products/components/ProductTile.tsx and is imported by every page that needs it. When we improve the tile (better photo cropping, smoother hover state) every product improves.

2 · A shared data layer

The PostgreSQL database has one products table per workspace. The POS reads it, the storefront reads it, the catalog reads it. There's no copy-paste between products, no synchronization lag, no data inconsistency. The downside is that one bad write to products shows up in every surface immediately — which is why audit trails and the permission model exist.

3 · Per-route gates

The URL is the configuration. /c/<slug>/pos/menu says: render the staff POS view. /menu/<slug> says: render the public storefront. Both URLs end up fetching from the same products endpoint, but the route gate decides which auth model applies (staff JWT vs. anonymous) and which UI to render.

The challenge is keeping the "shared library" abstraction honest: when a sibling chat builds a new product, they have to resist the temptation to copy-paste an existing component and "just tweak it a little." The discipline is to extract the difference as a prop, not fork the component. The SideCartPanel's "merge-safe" refactor is exactly that: its qty controls became optional props so the same panel works for the staff POS (with steppers) and the guest storefront (without).

The discipline also runs in reverse: when a fork slips through, it gets consolidated. The guest storefront originally shipped with its own module-local StorefrontShell for branding. Once the platform-wide BrandShell (+ useBrand) landed in the branding module, the storefront was refactored to nest all four of its steps under a single StorefrontLayout that renders the shared shell — the brand is fetched once per guest visit — and the local fork was deleted. One branding implementation now skins the workspace portal, the admin preview, and the public storefront.

The folder structure

What lives where in the repository — and, more importantly, why. This is the high-level system design you can see with ls.

Why a folder tree is architecture

The folder tree is the first diagram anyone sees — before any document, before any line of code. A good tree answers "what IS this system?" in one glance and "where would this change go?" in two. A bad one turns every task into a scavenger hunt. Because this document is also meant to teach software design, each part of this chapter leads with the transferable principle, then shows how ExSol applies it as the worked example.

The repository, top to bottom
Principle 1 — the top level of a repo should scream what the system IS

Before reading a single code file, a newcomer running ls at the root should see the system's shape: here is the app, here is the API, here is the database schema, here are the tests, here are the docs. Every top-level entry answers exactly one question — and there is no misc/, no old/, no mystery folder.

Principle 3 — shared infrastructure stays small, explicit, and boring

src/lib/ contains only what literally every module needs: the router, the admin auth context, the API client, Google sign-in, and the theme stylesheets. The rule: the moment something is needed by only some modules, it moves into a module (or modules/shared/). A fat shared layer is where feature-first structure goes to die — every casual "just put it in utils" quietly rebuilds the layer-first architecture you were trying to avoid.

Principle 4 — let the platform's constraints shape the backend layout

netlify/functions/ is deliberately flat: 138 endpoint files side by side, named <module>-<action>.ts. Netlify's discovery rule is "each top-level file is one function; a subdirectory is treated as ONE function" — so nesting endpoints in folders silently breaks routing. Rather than fight the platform, the convention embraces it: the module prefix in the filename provides the grouping a folder would have, and the underscore prefix (_shared/, _pos-authz.ts) marks "helper, not an endpoint" — Netlify skips it, and humans read it as internal.

The test of a good structure

Ask: "a bug in the booking calendar's timezone math — where's the fix?" src/modules/booking/, in the tz lib. "A new export format for the product catalog?" netlify/functions/_shared/exporters/. "A whole new module?" One folder under src/modules/, one manifest in the registry, a set of <module>-*.ts functions, one migration, one test folder. When every plausible change has one obvious home, the structure is doing its job.

Chapter 6 · Foundations

Load-bearing files & how they connect

The low-level system design in one chapter: the ~20 files that carry the architecture, and the four flows that run through them.

The idea of a load-bearing file

In a building, most walls just divide rooms; a few carry the structure. Codebases are the same. Of the 385 frontend files and 138 backend functions, most are leaf code — a page here, an endpoint there — that could be rewritten without anything else noticing. A small set of files, though, is load-bearing: every route, every request, every permission check passes through them. Know these twenty and you know the system.

The principle, then the list
Principle — a system is legible when you can name the files that carry it

If you can't write down the short list of files where the architecture lives, you don't have an architecture — you have a pile. Keeping the list short, and each file on it to a single responsibility, is a design activity, not documentation. When a load-bearing file grows a second job, that's the signal to split it before the list stops being writable.

Group 1 — Composition & routing

Group principle — one route table, one type source, one composition root
Scattering routes, shared types, or module registration across many files hides the product map. ExSol keeps each in exactly one file, so "what exists?" always has one answer.

Flow A — SPA route → gate · Principle: the client-side gate is UX, not security

Its whole job is to never show a link or screen the server would reject. Deleting every client gate wouldn't open a security hole — it would just be a slower, uglier way to see 403s.

1
The app composes itself. main.tsx mounts App.tsx: AuthProvider wraps RouterProvider, so auth state exists before any route renders.
2

3

4

5
All of that state came from one call. user-auth-context fetched GET /api/u-me once on mount — the frontend's entire authz picture is that single response.

The JWT proves who you are; the database says what you may do, right now. A stale or tampered claim can name a user, but it can never grant a permission.

1
Every function is a (Request) ⇒ Response. No framework, no middleware stack — just a function whose first act is calling the auth chain.
2
requirePermission tries the admin realm first; failing that, it verifies the bu_session JWT.
3
Authority is re-fetched, not trusted. For bucket users, level_number and client_id are fetched fresh from user_nodes — deliberately NOT read out of the JWT beyond identity.
4
The matrix decides. L1 bypasses it; L2+ needs the exact key in the level's client_levels JSONB matrix.
5
Tenancy is enforced. resolveClientId compares the requested tenant to the session's — a mismatch is a 403 forbidden_cross_client, regardless of permissions.
6
Product endpoints add the enable-gate. _<product>-authz.ts checks client_enabled_products; product off for this workspace → 412, before any permission logic.
7
Only now does db() run SQL — and every mutation ends with logAudit(). The client-side gates of Flow A were convenience; this chain is the security.
Flow C — Registry → everything · Principle: one composition root — adding a module is a declaration, not a scavenger hunt

Register once; everything else is derived. A fact declared once and derived N times cannot drift out of sync — the corollary is "derive, don't duplicate."

1
A Product declares its modules in its ProductManifest — plus requires dependencies and any legacy action permissions.
2

3

4

Flow D — Migrations, dev → prod · Principle: schema changes are forward-only code, applied by an operator, never by the build

The runner is deliberately dumb; the discipline lives in the ordering.

1
A schema change is a new numbered SQL file in db/migrations/ — never an edit to an applied one.
2
scripts/migrate.ts applies what's pending. It ensures schema_migrations exists, applies each pending file in a transaction, and records it — idempotent on re-run.
3
Dev first, then prod, by env swap. npm run migrate against the dev Neon branch; the same command against the prod URL — BEFORE deploying code that depends on the new schema (additive changes).
4
Destructive changes invert the order. Deploy the code that stops using the column first; only then drop it.

The Tenant Model

How one platform serves many businesses without leaking data between them.

First, an analogy

Think of a large coworking building. There are 200 companies inside, each in their own room. They all share the lobby, the elevators, the wifi, the cleaners. But you can't walk into another company's room and read their mail. The building's security model is: each room has a key, and your key only opens your room.

ExSol is the building. Each business — we call them a client or tenant or workspace (these all mean the same thing) — is one room. The data they create stays in their room. The other 199 businesses on the platform can never read it, even if they go poking at the URL.

In code

Every meaningful data row in the database has a client_id column. The application code, at every entry point, scopes every query by client_id. There is no Postgres Row-Level Security — tenancy is enforced at the application layer through three coordinated mechanisms:

  1. JWT cookie carries the tenant binding. When a staff member logs into a workspace, the cookie they receive contains their user_node_id AND their client_id. They cannot "log into" a different workspace without obtaining a different cookie. The cookie is signed; tampering would invalidate it.
  2. Every endpoint checks the URL's client param against the cookie. If a malicious user hand-edits a URL from /c/papa-s-saloon/pos/menu to /c/another-business/pos/menu and tries to load it, the backend sees: cookie says client=papa-s-saloon, URL says client=another-business, these don't match → return 403 forbidden_cross_client.
  3. Every SQL query is parameterized by client_id. The auth helper resolves the client_id from the cookie, hands it to the handler, and the handler uses it in every SQL query. There is no query that pulls data without a client_id filter.
What about admins?

Admins (the operators of ExSol itself — Faraaz and team) have a different cookie that grants cross-workspace access. Their authentication is separate (the admin login flow, the admin dashboard at /). When an admin acts on a specific workspace, they pass ?client=<uuid> explicitly in the URL — which is how the backend knows which workspace to target.

Bucket-users (workspace owners and staff) cannot pass ?client= — it's silently ignored or rejected if it mismatches their JWT. This asymmetry is the cross-client guard.

The Permission Model

How the system decides whether an action is allowed.

The everyday version

Imagine the workspace as an office with several rooms — products room, bookings room, files room. The Owner has master keys to every room. Each staff member gets a key ring with only the keys they need: maybe "products: can look at, can edit" but not "files: can delete." The set of keys is called their permission matrix, and it's stored once per level (L2's keys, L3's keys, etc.).

The technical model

Permissions are strings like products.products.view (module = products, bucket = products, verb = view). Each level in a workspace has a JSONB map of { "permissions.key": true } in the client_levels table. The auth chain looks up the level for the requesting user, consults this matrix, and either approves the action or returns 403.

Model A: Bucket × Verb

For CRUD-style operations (Create / Read / Update / Delete on a resource). Keys look like <module>.<bucket>.<verb>. 

products.products.create
products.categories.edit
booking.customers.view
booking.employees.edit
files.documents.delete
Used by ProductsBookingFilesTeam

Model B: Action namespace

For state-machine operations where "verbs on a resource" doesn't carve cleanly. Keys look like <module>.<action>.

pos.sale.refund
pos.history.viewAll
Used by POS
L1 Owner is all-on by default

Both models honor a special rule: if level_number === 1, skip the matrix check and grant every permission. This is what makes a fresh workspace usable on day one without configuring keys. The Owner can immediately log in and do everything. L2+ staff need explicit grants.

Chapter 10 · How a request works

End-to-end request lifecycle

What happens between a click in the browser and a row in the database.

Walking through it slowly

Let's follow a single click: a staff member at papa-s-saloon clicks "Mark paid" on order S-00002. Here's everything that happens, in order.

1
The browser sends a network request. A POST to /api/pos/sales/<id>/state with body { action: 'markPaid', paymentMethod: 'cash' } and the user's session cookie attached.
2
Netlify Edge receives it. Netlify is the platform we host on — it has servers all over the world. The request hits whichever edge node is closest to the user. The edge consults its function registry and figures out: this URL routes to the function file at netlify/functions/pos-sale-state.ts.
3
The function spins up. Netlify Functions are short-lived processes — they start fresh per request (or per small batch), run their code, return a response, and shut down. This is "serverless." The function file we wrote runs.
4
Auth chain runs. The function calls requirePos(req, ['pos.sale.markPaid']). That helper: reads the cookie, validates the JWT signature, looks up the user_node in the database, checks that POS is enabled for this workspace, checks the user holds the markPaid permission (skipped if they're L1 Owner), and either returns an authorized context or returns a 403/401 response.
5
The handler does the work. Validates the body with zod (rejects malformed input with 400), consults the FSM (is the current sale state pending_payment? if not, return 409), updates the database row inside a transaction, and — because the channel is "instore" — auto-advances the state from paid to fulfilled in the same call.
6
Audit log entry is written. A row is inserted into audit_log with op: 'pos.sale.markPaid', the actor's user_node_id, the target sale's id, and a JSON blob with the from/to states. This is the immutable history of what happened.
7
Response goes back. The handler returns jsonOk({ sale: updatedRow }). Netlify Edge forwards it to the browser. The browser's React code receives the JSON, updates the page (in theory — there's an open UX bug where it doesn't auto-refetch, see operational cliffs).
Total time: ~200-400ms

A well-behaved POS state transition runs end-to-end in about a quarter to half a second. Most of that is network latency, not computation. The actual database write is <5ms; the function cold-start (first request after idle) adds 100-200ms; subsequent requests in the same minute are warm.

The Data Model

The shape of the database, and why it's shaped that way.

A short orientation

The database is one PostgreSQL instance with 29 tables (the exact, always-current list lives in the generated docs/reference/schema.md). Every table either belongs to a workspace (via client_id) or is platform-level (the admins table is the only one). Conceptually, the tables form a tree: clients is the root, and most other tables hang off it.

The core tables, by group

Identity

  • admins — ExSol team members. Cross-workspace.
  • clients — workspaces. One row per business. Has a slug (URL identifier) and a name.
  • user_nodes — every person inside a workspace: Owner, staff, customers. Has a level_number, a parent_id (for L2 reporting to L1), and a role_id.
  • user_node_credentials — login credentials for any user_node that needs them (Owner, staff). Customers don't have credentials unless they sign up.
  • client_levels — the permission matrix per level per workspace. Stored as JSONB.
  • client_roles — named role labels (Owner, Cashier, Stylist) with optional color and bucket_family.

Configuration

  • client_enabled_products — which modules each workspace has turned on. A workspace without "pos" enabled here cannot use the POS module even if their code path tries.
  • client_cardinality_rules — limits like "max 1 user per L1" — used by AMS for tier policies.

Products (catalog)

  • products — the catalog. Has name, type, price_cents, optional sale_price_cents, discount_percent, status (active / draft / archived), pos_visible, storefront_visible, and a long tail of Phase B columns (sku, gtin, category, brand, etc.). 49 columns total.
  • product_categories — categorization tree.
  • product_images — image files attached to products, stored in Netlify Blobs with thumb pipeline.

Sales (orders)

  • sales — order header. order_no (per-bucket sequence), status (FSM enum), channel (instore / pickup / online), source (storefront / instore / pickup / online), customer info (plain text, no FK to a customer table), payment_ref (idempotency key + future Razorpay id), payment_method, paid_at, fulfilled_at, cancelled_at, refunded_at.
  • sale_lines — one row per line in the cart, with snapshot prices so future product edits don't rewrite history.

Bookings

  • booking_settings — per-workspace timezone, weekly schedule, slot interval, cancellation cutoff.
  • booking_resources — named resources (Stylist A, Chair 2). Active flag.
  • booking_resource_time_off — vacation/blocks per resource.
  • booking_services — services offered (Haircut, Color), with duration, price, payment_mode (pay_at_venue / deposit / full_upfront).
  • bookings — the actual appointment. resource_id, service_id, customer_user_node_id (nullable for blocked time), time_range (tstzrange), status enum, manage_token. Has a GIST exclusion constraint preventing two bookings overlapping on the same resource.

Stock (Inventory + Warehouse)

  • inventory_stock — one row per (workspace, product): qty_on_hand, reorder_level. The cached rollup; never negative (CHECK-enforced).
  • stock_movements — the append-only ledger. qty_delta, a typed reason (sale / purchase / adjustment / production / transfer, a Postgres enum), a free-text ref (sale:<id>, po:<id>), and who did it. No update trigger, no UPDATE anywhere in code.
  • warehouse_locations — named places stock can sit (warehouse / store / storage / other), unique per workspace.
  • stock_by_location — the per-location breakdown, layered over inventory_stock; a transfer moves qty between locations without changing the product's total on hand.

Buying & the books (Procurement + Finance)

  • suppliers — who we buy from; soft-deleted (deleted_at) so old purchase orders keep their supplier.
  • purchase_orders + purchase_order_items — header with a 4-state FSM (draft / ordered / received / cancelled) + snapshot lines (qty, unit_cost_cents).
  • finance_expenses — the expenses ledger: a constrained category set, amount_cents ≥ 0, incurred_on DATE. Deliberately expenses-only — revenue is read live from sales and bookings, never copied.
  • email_outbox — one row per send attempt: template, payload, rendered body, and a status (pending / sent / logged / failed). The record of every email the platform tried to send.

Files

  • files — uploaded documents (PDFs, photos, videos). Per-client storage path in Netlify Blobs.
  • file_categories — finance/HR/legal/etc. taxonomy.
  • file_audience — who can see this file (role / node / user grants).
  • workspace_storage_quotas — per-client byte limit + current usage cache.

Campaigns & time (Marketing + Workforce depth)

  • marketing_campaigns — one row per campaign: subject, body, audience (all / recent_30d), status (draft → sent). The audience is a query over crm_customers, resolved at send time.
  • campaign_sends — one row per recipient per campaign; the campaign-side ledger that pairs with email_outbox's delivery record.
  • timesheet_entries (107) — billable time logged by a user against a booking resource, with an approval workflow (approved_by, approved_at). The first depth-phase table.

Audit

  • audit_log — every mutation writes a row. actor_admin OR actor_user_node, op (e.g. pos.sale.markPaid), client_id, target_type, target_id, detail JSONB. Cross-cutting; not owned by any single module.
Key design decisions you should know
  • Snapshot prices on sale_lines and product_name on sale_lines. When a sale is created, we copy the product name and unit price into the line row. Future edits to the product don't change history.
  • customer_name and customer_phone as plain text on sales. No foreign key to a customer table. Why: guest sales would otherwise force every guest to create a record. Future CRM integration can match-or-link these post-hoc.
  • tstzrange + GIST exclusion on bookings. Concurrent requests for the same slot can't both succeed because the database itself enforces the no-overlap rule. The application doesn't need to "lock" anything.
  • JSONB for permission matrices. Permissions vary in shape per module; a rigid relational model would be painful. JSONB gives flexibility at the cost of slightly more complex queries.
  • Stock truth is a ledger, not a number. stock_movements is append-only; inventory_stock.qty_on_hand is the rollup, updated in the same transaction as each movement. You can always answer "why is the count 7?" by reading the movements.
  • DATE columns are serialized as text. The Neon driver turns a bare Postgres date into a JS Date at local midnight, which can shift a day when serialized as UTC. Finance returns incurred_on via to_char(incurred_on, 'YYYY-MM-DD') instead — a one-line fix that took a real bug to learn.

State machines

Why most operational records have a well-defined lifecycle and what we get for the trouble.

Why states matter

Take a POS sale. It can be pending payment, paid, fulfilled, cancelled, or refunded. Each transition between these states is a business event with its own rules: you can refund a paid sale, but you can't refund a cancelled one. You can mark fulfilled from paid, but not from pending. If we didn't encode these rules, a careless click could put a sale into a nonsense state.

A finite state machine (FSM) is the formal name for "a set of states + the rules about which transitions are legal." We use FSMs for sales (5 states), bookings (6 states), purchase orders (4 states: draft / ordered / received / cancelled), and most operational records.

The POS sale FSM
pending_payment → paid       (action: markPaid)
pending_payment → cancelled  (action: cancel)
paid → fulfilled             (action: fulfill — auto if channel='instore')
paid → refunded              (action: refund)
fulfilled → refunded         (action: refund)

// channel-aware: instore auto-fulfills, pickup requires two clicks

The FSM is enforced server-side in the handler before any database write. The client UI also reads the current state and shows only the legal action buttons (so the user can't even click "fulfill" when the sale is still pending), but the server check is the authority — a malicious client could forge the request and the server would still reject illegal transitions with 409.

The booking FSM is similar but channel-aware in a different way
Per-service payment policy decides whether a booking starts at pending_payment or confirmed. payment: 'none' → confirmed immediately. payment: 'required' → pending_payment, with a cleanup cron that cancels stale bookings after 15 min. payment: 'optional' behaves like 'none' but allows post-hoc payment at the counter.

Audit trail

How we know what happened, who did it, and when.

Why every business needs an audit log

A customer complains: "I ordered three coffees, you charged me for five." The Owner asks: "Who took the order? When? What did they enter?" Without an audit log, there's no answer. With one: a few SQL queries and you can reconstruct the exact sequence of events.

Audit logs are also legal protection (for disputes), operational insight (was the storefront slow because someone was hammering it?), and a debugging aid (this row looks wrong — who edited it?).

The audit_log table is append-only by convention (no UPDATEs, no DELETEs in normal use). Every mutation across the platform writes a row via the logAudit(session, op, { target, detail }) helper. The detail JSONB blob is module-specific: 

// Example: storefront sale created by an anonymous guest
{
  occurred_at: '2026-06-30T14:48:18Z',
  op: 'pos.sale.created',
  actor_admin: null,
  actor_user_node: null,  // both null = anonymous guest
  client_id: 'a9c733b4-...',
  target_type: 'sale',
  target_id: '050e97fc-...',
  detail: {
    lines: 1,
    source: 'storefront',
    channel: 'pickup',

}

What makes this powerful: the op namespace is structured (module.entity.action), so you can ask "all POS state transitions in the last 24 hours" with a simple WHERE op LIKE 'pos.sale.%'. The detail JSONB captures whatever the module thinks is interesting at the moment — for state transitions, the from/to states; for exports, the byte count + table counts; for FSM auto-transitions, a flag like auto: true so you can distinguish "staff clicked twice" from "system auto-advanced."

The email module takes the opposite route: no standalone product; it's unlocked through the products that use it.

The "products require products" wording is a quirk: posProduct.requires = ['products'] means "the POS product requires the Products product to also be enabled for the workspace." The POS reads the catalog from the Products module, so enabling POS without Products would leave the POS with nothing to sell.

The registry grew a new job: navigation

For most of the platform's life, the workspace sidebar knew about modules the hard way: a hand-maintained list of which modules had their own nav section, plus one hand-written block of JSX per module. Every new module meant editing the sidebar again — and forgetting meant an invisible module.

Decision → debt → payoff

The decision (early): hard-code nav in the Sidebar. At 4 modules this was the right call — an abstraction would have been speculative. The debt (by module 19): a MODULES_WITH_DEDICATED_NAV set plus 15 near-identical gates, each a copy-paste-edit, each a chance to drift from the registry. The payoff (2026-07-06, commit 639daae): ModuleManifest gained hasDedicatedNav and navLinks; the Sidebar became one loop over the manifests; the set and all 15 gates were deleted. Nav joined the permission matrix and enabled_modules as the third thing derived from the registry (Flow C) — which is the general lesson: when a duplication is young, tolerate it; when it has 15 copies, promote it into the composition root and delete the copies.

Chapter 16 · How modules are built

API patterns

What every backend function looks like, structurally.

Every Netlify Function in this repo follows the same skeleton. Once you can read one, you can read all 138 of them. The shape is: 

// netlify/functions/<name>.ts
import type { Context } from '@netlify/functions';
import { jsonError, jsonOk } from './_shared/http';
import { requirePos } from './_pos-authz';
import { logAudit } from './_shared/audit';
import { db } from './_shared/db';
import { SaleStateBody } from './_pos-validators';

export const config = { path: '/api/pos/sales/:id/state', method: 'POST' };

export default async (req: Request, _ctx: Context) => {
  // 1. Auth + perm gate (returns Response on failure)
  const auth = await requirePos(req, ['pos.sale.markPaid']);
  if (!auth.ok) return auth.res;

  // 2. Validate body with zod
  const body = SaleStateBody.safeParse(await req.json());
  if (!body.success) return jsonError(400, 'invalid_body', { details: body.error });

  // 3. Do the work (FSM transition, channel-aware auto-fulfill)
  const sql = db();
  const [updated] = await sql`UPDATE sales SET status = ..., paid_at = now() WHERE ...`;

  // 4. Audit
  await logAudit(auth.ctx, 'pos.sale.markPaid', {
    target_id: updated.id,
    detail: { from: 'pending_payment', to: 'paid' },
  });

  return jsonOk(updated);
};

Auth → validate → work → audit → respond. Always in that order. The auth helper is module-specific (requirePos for POS, requireBooking for booking) but they all share the same pattern: enable-gate first, then permission check, with the L1 Owner bypass after the enable-gate.

Chapter 17 · How modules are built

The first ERP wave — five modules

One integration wave — migrations 052 through 057 — took four rows of the ERP table from "planned" to working v1 software, and added the email infrastructure underneath. Each module was built by its own parallel session against the anatomy of Chapter 14, then merged and smoke-tested together. (A second wave has since repeated the trick — that's the next chapter.)

Why these five, together

They form a chain. Inventory answers "what do we have?" — the first question every physical business asks. Procurement answers "how does more arrive?" and proves stock can go up through the same ledger that sales push down. Warehouse answers "where is it?" without inventing a second stock system. Finance answers "did we make money?" by reading the sales and bookings that already exist. And Email is the quiet plumbing the others — and Marketing Automation later — speak through.

Module by module

Inventory (migration 053) — stock truth

Two tables. inventory_stock holds one row per (workspace, product): quantity on hand and a reorder level, with a CHECK that the count can never go negative. stock_movements is the append-only ledger: every change to stock is an INSERT with a signed qty_delta and a typed reason — sale, purchase, adjustment, production, transfer (a Postgres enum, so an unknown type can't even be written). There is no UPDATE of a movement anywhere in the codebase.

Golden flow: a cashier fulfils a POS sale → pos-sale-state.ts decrements inventory_stock per sale line and appends a type='sale' movement (ref = sale:<id>) — but only if the workspace opted in via clients.inventory_tracking_enabled, and fail-open: the hook is wrapped so an inventory error can never block taking money. Staff view stock and adjust it (with a reason) at /c/<slug>/inventory.

What it seeds: Manufacturing (the production movement type reserved here is now in live use — Chapter 18) and the Supply Chain dashboard (which reads the ledger, not new tables).

Email & Notifications (migration 052) — the mailer seam

One helper, one table. _shared/mailer.ts exposes sendMail(), which writes an email_outbox row first (status pending), renders the template, delivers through a thin provider adapter (_shared/resend.ts — a raw fetch call, no SDK dependency), and reconciles the row to sent, logged, or failed. The helper never throws: an email problem cannot break the booking or sale it announces.

Golden flow: a guest books online or checks out on the storefront → the create endpoint calls sendMail() → a confirmation (with a calendar .ics attachment, for bookings) or a receipt goes out. If RESEND_API_KEY isn't set, nothing leaves the machine — the row is recorded as logged, which is how dev and demo environments run without a provider account. The outbox is browsable at /c/<slug>/email.

What it seeded — now cashed in: Marketing Automation (#13) shipped exactly as predicted: a campaign is "many sendMail calls over a CRM audience," and the outbox is its delivery record (see the third-wave chapter).

Finance (migration 054) — a money-legible P&L

One deliberately small table: finance_expenses (a constrained category set — rent, utilities, salaries, … — amount in cents, an incurred_on date). Revenue gets no table at all: the monthly summary endpoint computes it live — POS + storefront revenue from sales (paid/fulfilled), booking revenue from bookings (confirmed/completed), minus the month's expenses = net. Because revenue is never copied, Finance can't drift out of sync with the order books.

Golden flow: the owner logs an expense → it appears in the ledger and the month's P&L moves, at /c/<slug>/finance. A teaching detail: incurred_on is returned as to_char(…, 'YYYY-MM-DD') because the Neon driver otherwise turns a bare DATE into a local-midnight timestamp that can shift a day in transit — a real bug, fixed the honest way.

What it seeds: AR/AP, payment ledgers, and tax inputs — all extensions of the same read-model-over-existing-truth approach.

Procurement (migration 056) — stock goes up

suppliers (soft-deleted, so history keeps its names) plus purchase_orders with snapshot line items. Orders move through a 4-state FSM — draft → ordered → received, or cancelled — with illegal transitions rejected as 409, exactly like POS sales.

Golden flow: the receive transition is the module's reason to exist: in one database transaction it upserts inventory_stock (+qty per line), appends a type='purchase' movement per line (ref = po:<id>), and stamps the order received. This is the first module to write into another module's domain — and it does so through the movements ledger, not by reaching into Inventory's endpoints.

What it seeds: the Supply Chain dashboard (procurement → inventory → orders is now a queryable chain) and reorder automation (reorder_level + supplier catalog are both in place).

Warehouse (migration 057) — where stock sits

A locations layer over Inventory, not a second stock system. warehouse_locations names the places (warehouse, store, storage); stock_by_location breaks the product's total down per place. A transfer, in one transaction, decrements the source location, increments the destination, and writes two type='transfer' movements (−qty and +qty) — net-zero, so the product's total on hand is untouched by design.

Golden flow: move 10 units from "Back storage" to "Store floor" at /c/<slug>/warehouse; the ledger records both sides, and an over-transfer is rejected by the database CHECK (surfaced as insufficient_stock), not by hopeful application code.

What it seeds: bin/lot tracking and pick lists — finer-grained "where," same ledger.

The principle the wave proves — one ledger, many writers

Four modules (POS, Inventory, Procurement, Warehouse) changed stock at the end of this wave, and none of them invented a private mechanism: every change is an INSERT into stock_movements plus an atomic update of the rollup. Manufacturing has since landed as exactly the predicted fifth writer — with zero new read paths (Chapter 18). Compare that with the alternative — each module keeping its own count and a nightly job reconciling them — and you have the clearest argument in this codebase for designing the shared fact first and the modules around it.

Honest status

These are v1s, deliberately narrow: Inventory has no lot/expiry tracking; Finance has no AR/AP; Procurement has no RFQs; Warehouse has no pick lists. Each shipped the smallest slice that creates real operational truth, wired into the modules that already exist. The modules that were "in development on parallel branches" when this chapter was first written — CRM (055), Manufacturing (058), Workforce + Project Service (059) — have since merged; the next chapter covers them.

Chapter 18 · How modules are built

The second wave — six modules, five merges

Migrations 055, 058, 059, 061 and 062 — five merges that added six module folders (CRM, Manufacturing, Workforce + Project Service, Data Collection, Catalog, Brand Portfolio) and completed the seven-product suite. Same recipe as the first wave: one parallel session per module, the anatomy of Chapter 14, the per-module authz-gate pattern of Chapter 6, merged and smoke-tested on main.

Why these six, together

The first wave built the operational spine — what do we have, how does more arrive, where is it, did we make money. This wave answers the people and public questions: who are our customers (CRM), who works when and on what (Workforce + Project Service), what are we making (Manufacturing) — and then opens three public doors: a token-gated onboarding flow for new clients, a no-purchase catalog site, and a per-brand portfolio site. With those three doors, every product in the Chapter 2 table is live.

Module by module

CRM (migration 055) — who buys from us

Two tables. crm_customers is a read-model: one row per real-world customer, deduped by a phone/email dedupe_key (unique per workspace), tagged with a source (pos / storefront / booking) and a last_seen timestamp. crm_notes holds staff notes against a customer. Nothing here is a second source of truth — the truth stays in sales and bookings.

Golden flow: POST /api/crm/refresh re-projects the read-model — a pure merge lib scans sales + bookings, collapses them by dedupe key, and upserts crm_customers. The list page at /c/<slug>/crm shows every customer across every channel; the detail page unifies their orders, bookings, and notes into one activity timeline.

What it seeds: Marketing Automation's audience segmentation (a campaign is "a query over crm_customers plus many sendMail calls") and Project Service's customer links, which arrived one migration later.

A design contrast worth studying — two answers to "is this the same person?"

The suite answers the same person-question twice, in opposite ways, and both are right. Employees are shared identity: Workforce points a shift at a user_nodes row by foreign key, because an employee carries authorization — level, permissions, login — and that must have exactly one authoritative row. Fork it and the copies disagree about what someone may do. Customers are a projected read-model: CRM does not FK sales and bookings to a customer master; it periodically projects them into crm_customers, because a customer carries only history, and guest checkout must never require creating an account first. The principle: share identity when it grants authority; project it when it only accumulates history.

Workforce + Project Service (migration 059) — who works, on what

Three tables, two modules, one migration. workforce_shifts lays a weekly schedule over Booking's named resources: each shift binds a booking_resources row to a weekday (0–6) and a time range (CHECK: end after start), optionally tied to a real team member via user_node_id. projects (quoted → active → done) links a job to a crm_customers row; project_assignments puts named resources on the job.

Golden flow: the owner lays out the week per resource at /c/<slug>/workforce; a services business opens a project for a CRM customer at /workforce/projects, assigns the resources, and advances it quoted → active → done. Note the reuse: no new "employee" or "calendar" primitive was invented — shifts ride the same resources the booking widget already books against.

What it seeds: timesheets and attendance (shifts are the schedule half; actuals are the follow-up) and billable hours per project.

Manufacturing (migration 058) — making things

Three tables. boms names an output product; bom_components lists what goes into one unit (component product + qty, CHECK > 0); production_orders runs a four-state FSM — planned → in_progress → done, or cancelled — enforced by a Postgres enum, illegal transitions rejected exactly like POS sales.

Golden flow: completing an order is the module's reason to exist: in one database transaction it appends type='production' movements — negative for each component consumed, positive for the finished output — and updates the stock rollups; insufficient components reject the completion. This makes Manufacturing the fifth writer through the movements ledger, the one Chapter 17 predicted, and it means all five movement types (sale · purchase · adjustment · transfer · production) now have real writers.

What it seeds: the Supply Chain dashboard (procurement → manufacturing → inventory → orders is now a fully queryable chain — its read-projection has since shipped, see the third-wave chapter) and production costing.

Data Collection App (migration 061) — the front door for new clients

One tiny table: onboard_tokens — a single-use token per row, with expires_at bounding its life and used_at marking it consumed. No new product tables: the intake writes into the same products catalog everything else reads.

Golden flow: an operator mints a token from the workspace UI → the new client opens /onboard/<token> — a public page with no login; token possession is the authorization (unknown → 404, used or expired → 410) — and bulk-loads their catalog. The moment the products land, the POS, storefront, and catalog site can all sell/show them.

What it seeds: the rest of onboarding — the same token pattern extends to any pre-login intake step (hours, branding, staff lists).

Catalog Website (migration 061) — the no-cart storefront

Zero new tables. One public endpoint (pub-catalog) serves /catalog/<slug>, and the page reuses the storefront's browsing components with the cart and checkout removed — "Add to cart" becomes a contact link.

Golden flow: a workspace flips the catalog product on → their existing storefront_visible products render as a showcase site, immediately, with no data entry. The cheapest product in the suite to ship, precisely because Chapter 4's shared-component discipline held: the difference really was a prop, not a fork.

What it seeds: SEO-facing public pages generally — and the browsing half of any future marketplace surface.

Brand Portfolio Sites (migration 062) — one brand, one site

One table: brand_site_config, one row per client — a sections JSONB holding per-section enable flags and copy, plus a published boolean. Sections compose suite components (hero, product grid, contact) wired to the client's live data.

Golden flow: the owner assembles their site in the internal editor at /c/<slug>/brand-site, toggles sections, and hits publish → the public site renders at /site/<slug> (and only when published). Because sections read live workspace data, a price change in the catalog updates the brand site with no republish.

What it seeds: custom domains (the Chapter 1.9 host-resolution plan in the sibling document) and richer section kits.

Honest status

Same deal as the first wave — narrow v1s: CRM has no leads or opportunities pipeline; Manufacturing has no work-in-progress costing; Catalog and Portfolio sites have no custom domains yet. The two modules that were "in motion" when this chapter was first written — Supply Chain and Marketing Automation — have since merged, and Workforce's missing timesheets shipped as the first depth slice. The next chapter covers all three, which closed out the width phase entirely.

Chapter 20 · Operations

Deployment

How code goes from a developer's laptop to a customer's screen.

The non-technical version

We use Netlify, a service that takes our code from GitHub, builds the website + backend, and runs it on their servers. Every time someone with permission pushes new code to the main branch on GitHub, Netlify notices, runs the build, and replaces the live version with the new one. No manual server management; no SSH; no "did the deploy work" anxiety beyond a "ready" signal in the Netlify dashboard.

The database is hosted on a different service called Neon — they run PostgreSQL, and we have two separate "branches" (think: copies) of our database: one for development, one for production. Changing the database structure (called a "migration") is a manual step done before pushing code that depends on the change.

The full sequence for a production push
  1. Verify the prod Neon endpoint is the right one: echo $PROD_DB_URL | grep dawn-bird. If we get this wrong and run a destructive migration on the dev database thinking it's prod, we'd silently break the dev workflow.
  2. For additive migrations (new tables, new columns): run npm run migrate against prod FIRST. This is because code that expects the column would crash if the column doesn't exist yet. Once the column is there, code that doesn't yet use it is fine — additive migrations are forward-compatible.
  3. For destructive migrations (dropping columns, dropping tables): run code FIRST. This is the opposite of additive — destructive migrations are backward-incompatible. You want the old code that uses the column to stop running before you drop the column.
  4. git push origin main. Netlify auto-builds (~30-50s) and auto-deploys.
  5. Watch for the "ready" state. Probe new endpoints — if they 404 with SPA HTML, the Edge function registry hasn't picked them up yet (this is a known trap — see operational cliffs). Run netlify api restoreSiteDeploy to nudge it.
  6. If the alias-not-promoted trap fires (alias URL serves the old bundle hash while per-deploy URL serves the new one), restoreSiteDeploy again.
  7. UI smoke on a real workspace. Verify the new feature actually works for an end user, not just that the deploy went green.
Chapter 21 · Operations

Database migrations

How we evolve the database schema over time without losing data.

Migrations are numbered SQL files in db/migrations/. Each one is a small set of CREATE TABLE, ALTER TABLE, CREATE INDEX statements that describe a change. They're applied in numeric order (001, 002, …). Once applied, a row is inserted into schema_migrations so the runner knows to skip it next time.

Today the repo has 61 migration files: 001 through 062, plus 107. The names are descriptive: 038_products_discount_percent.sql adds a discount percent column; 040_sales.sql creates the sales table; 053_inventory.sql creates the stock ledger; 060_marketing.sql adds campaigns; 107_timesheet_entries.sql adds the first depth-phase table.

Parallel-development friction surfaces when two sibling chats both reserve the same migration number. Early on, the POS-v2 storefront work, the Booking work, and the File Manager Phase B work all branched independently and each tried to claim 043/044/045. We resolved by applying POS-v2's first, renumbering Booking's to 047/048/049, and File Manager landed cleanly at 046 (the gap we'd left). The feedback_booking_migration_number_coordination memory documents this.

The lesson became a rule: migration numbers are now allocated centrally by the human coordinator before a branch starts, never picked by the branch itself. That's why the sequence on main shows one honest gap in the width range — 051, reserved to Payments (on hold pending Razorpay keys). Gaps are coordination, not loss: 060, once reserved to the in-flight Marketing branch, filled in exactly on schedule when that branch merged, as CRM's 055 had before it.

Why the numbering suddenly jumps from 062 to 107

A reviewer listing the folder sees 062_brand_site_config.sql and then 107_timesheet_entries.sql and reasonably wonders where forty-four files went. Nowhere: they never existed.

Reserved per-module ranges

With the width phase done, one-number-at-a-time allocation stopped scaling: the depth phase runs many parallel sessions, each deepening a different module, each needing migration numbers without waiting on the coordinator per file. So the block 063–139 was pre-allocated as per-module bands — each module owns a contiguous range it can draw from freely. 107 is the first draw, from the Project-Manager/Workforce band (the file's own header records "depth range 107–111"). The invariant that matters — no two branches ever hold the same number — is preserved; what changed is the granularity of the reservation, from single numbers to ranges. Read 063–106 as allocated-but-unclaimed, not missing: schema_migrations has never recorded anything in that span.

Chapter 22 · Operations

Operational cliffs

Things that have gone wrong, written down so they don't go wrong again.

Every system accumulates a list of subtle failure modes that you only learn by hitting them. ExSol has a handful, all documented as memory entries and many in the architecture diagrams. The most important five:

The new-function-404 trap

Symptom: a new Netlify Function is in the deploy's function manifest, but probing its URL returns the SPA's 404 HTML instead of the function's response. Cause: Edge function routing registry hasn't caught up with the new function. Fix: netlify api restoreSiteDeploy nudges it. Always probe new endpoints after deploying them; never assume "ready" state means the routing is registered.

The alias-not-promoted trap

Symptom: the build produces a new JS / CSS bundle hash, but the workspace alias keeps serving HTML that references the previous hash. The new code is technically deployed but nobody is loading it. Detection: compare the bundle hash on the per-deploy URL vs. the alias URL. Fix: restoreSiteDeploy. Today's session hit this 4 times.

The browser-cache trap

Symptom: after a successful deploy, an open browser tab still loads the previous bundle (the HTML it loaded is cached in the browser, and that HTML references the old hash). Detection: compare document.querySelector('script[src*="/assets/"]').src in the open tab vs. curl /. Fix: hard reload. Always tell users after a deploy: refresh your tabs.

The L1-Owner bypass omission

Symptom: Owner of a workspace tries to use a newly-shipped module and gets blank UI or 403 errors, even though they "should" have access. Cause: the module's auth helper does a strict permission matrix check without the L1 bypass. Default-empty matrix means L1 Owner has no keys, so they're denied. Fix: add if (level_number === 1) return full-perm-set after the module enable-gate. Mirrors requirePos as the reference. Has now shipped 3 times (POS Phase 1, Booking, public storefront helpers) — memory entry feedback_module_l1_bypass_pattern documents the fix.

Function-shape collisions and naming

Three related ones to watch: (1) Two functions can't share the same config.path without config.method to discriminate (one will silently lose at the Edge layer). (2) Functions in subdirectories netlify/functions/foo/ are NOT auto-discovered as separate functions — the whole folder is treated as ONE function. Flat layout only: netlify/functions/foo-action.ts. (3) Integration tests bypass Netlify's routing, so a test suite can pass while routing is misconfigured.

What happens on every protected request

Chapter 6's Flow B is the security spine; here is the same chain read as a defense-in-depth list — each layer assumes the one before it failed.

Current — the enforcement chain
  1. Enable-gate first (412). _<module>-authz.ts checks client_enabled_products; a module the workspace hasn't enabled answers 412 before any permission logic runs. Only after that comes the level_number === 1 Owner bypass, then the matrix lookup — this exact order is iron rule 2, learned by shipping it wrong twice.
  2.   3. Input validation. Request bodies are Zod-parsed at the handler boundary — malformed input dies as a 400 before touching business logic.
  4. Append-only audit. Every mutation ends with logAudit() — one INSERT helper is the table's only writer; no handler anywhere UPDATEs or DELETEs audit_log. The audit write can fail without rolling back the business operation (recording history must not break the present).
  5. Fail-fast environment. The env itself is Zod-validated at boot: no DATABASE_URL, or a JWT_SIGNING_SECRET under 32 characters, refuses to start. Deliberately, the signing secret has no dev fallback; only soft dependencies degrade (no RESEND_API_KEY → outbox rows marked logged, nothing sent).
  6. Webhook signatures. The one live payment-adjacent endpoint, booking-razorpay-webhook.ts, rejects payloads that fail HMAC-SHA256 verification (timingSafeEqual) before reading them.
  7. Human-only releases. A committed PreToolUse hook (.claude/hooks/block-git-push.py) makes it impossible for any AI session to git push; every deploy passes through the human coordinator.
A deliberate decision, stated plainly — application-layer tenancy, no RLS

PostgreSQL offers Row-Level Security: policies inside the database that filter rows per user, a backstop even if application code forgets a WHERE client_id =. ExSol does not use it — zero CREATE POLICY statements exist in the schema. The reasoning: RLS wants a database role or session variable per request, which fits awkwardly with a serverless HTTP driver and a single shared connection function; and a second enforcement layer that most requests never exercise tends to rot unnoticed. Instead, tenancy is enforced once, uniformly, in the helper chain above — and the compensating control is uniformity itself: every endpoint composes the same helpers in the same order, so an endpoint that looks different is an endpoint to review, and the conformance audit checks exactly that. The trade-off is real: a forgotten filter in a hand-written query has no database backstop. If the platform ever lands on a host where RLS is idiomatic (Supabase — next chapters), this decision gets revisited rather than defended.

Portability & alternatives — the seam inventory as exit strategy

Every external dependency sits behind one owned file. This chapter prices the exits.

Why a document about leaving your own stack

Two audiences ask the same question differently. A technical evaluator asks: "how locked-in is this?" A prospective client asks: "we're a Supabase shop / we can't use Razorpay / we need an iOS app — can your platform meet us?" Both get the same answer: every external dependency is accessed through a single seam file that ExSol owns, so each swap has a known location and a known cost class. The inventory below was verified seam-by-seam against the code.

The principle
Principle — we don't promise every stack; we promise a priced exit from every dependency

A dependency reached from one file costs what one file costs to rewrite — hours or days. A dependency smeared across 138 handlers costs a migration project — weeks, with regressions. The architecture keeps every vendor in the first shape, which means the honest sales answer is never "we can't" and never "sure, free" — it's a number.

Database — the deepest dependency, three different exits

"Can we use our own database?" has three genuinely different answers depending on what "our own" means, and pretending they cost the same would be dishonest.

Postgres-to-Postgres · Postgres-to-MySQL · Postgres-to-document
  • Another Postgres host — hours. The entire request path builds its client in one file (_shared/db.ts, currently the Neon HTTP driver); everything downstream receives a sql tag function. Moving to Supabase, RDS, or Cloud SQL is a connection-string swap plus, at most, exchanging the Neon driver for pg inside that file. Supabase is the strategically interesting one: it wins for clients who want a bundled backend-as-a-service — RLS, realtime, storage, and auth in one console — and, crucially, adopting Supabase-Postgres does not force adopting its auth or storage: ExSol's owned session layer and storage wrappers run unchanged against a vanilla Postgres URL. Take the database; keep the seams.
  • MySQL / MariaDB — weeks, and here is exactly why. The code uses Postgres as Postgres, and the blockers are enumerable from the schema: the GiST exclusion constraint over a TSTZRANGE that makes double-booking atomically impossible (migration 048) has no MySQL equivalent — it would become application-level locking, a genuine correctness regression, not an inconvenience; ten JSONB columns across eight tables, including the permission matrix itself; native ENUM types guarding every FSM; RETURNING in 58 function files; ON CONFLICT upserts in 12; to_char date formatting in 14; partial indexes. Each is individually translatable; together they are a dialect-migration project. We quote it as weeks and recommend against it unless the mandate is immovable.
  • MongoDB or any document store — a considered rejection, not a gap. The schema is load-bearingly relational: 64 foreign-key references across 26 migrations, three append-only ledgers (stock_movements, audit_log, email_outbox) whose integrity IS the product's trustworthiness, a self-referencing org tree, and invariants (CHECK constraints, exclusion constraints, multi-table transactions) enforced by the database rather than by hope. Porting to Mongo means re-implementing those guarantees in application code — a data-layer rewrite sold as a migration. The honest answer to "can it run on Mongo?" is: it could be made to, and we decline, because the parts a client loves most — stock that always reconciles, bookings that never double-book — are precisely the parts the relational engine provides.
Auth — add a provider, or hand the whole layer away

Two very different client requests share this seam: "our staff live on iPhones / Microsoft 365" (add a provider) and "we need SSO with SOC2 evidence by Q3" (outsource the layer).

The seam and the two moves

All session logic — mint, verify, both realms — lives in _shared/session.ts; Google verification is isolated in google-verifier.ts, the only file importing google-auth-library; argon2 hashing in argon.ts. Adding Apple Sign-In is days: a sibling apple-verifier.ts validating Apple's token, feeding the same session mint — needed for iOS-heavy client bases, and mandatory the day a native iOS wrapper ships third-party sign-in (Apple policy; see the previous chapter). Microsoft / Entra SSO is the same pattern. Outsourcing to Auth0 / Clerk / Supabase Auth replaces session.ts's internals with the provider's SDK while requirePermission and everything above it stay put. The trade is explicit: the owned layer is a few hundred auditable lines and free at any scale; the hosted product buys federation, MFA, and compliance checkboxes fast, at per-MAU pricing and a third party inside the most sensitive path. The seam keeps both answers available per deployment — that optionality is the asset.

Hosting — the accidental masterstroke

The single biggest portability asset wasn't designed as one: Netlify Functions v2 adopted the web standard, so every handler is a plain (Request) ⇒ Response function — the exact signature Cloudflare Workers, Vercel Edge, and Deno Deploy execute.

What is actually Netlify-shaped

Three things: function discovery (flat files in one folder), routing (config.path/method exports plus the /api/* redirect), and Blobs (below). The @netlify/functions import in handlers is type-only. A port is therefore a router shim — one table mapping paths to the already-portable handlers — plus the storage swap: days to a week, handler bodies untouched. Storage itself is behind four _shared wrapper modules (files, product images, thumbnails, brand assets) plus two rate-limit helpers; no handler imports @netlify/blobs directly, and every test that touches storage already mocks the seam — the suite proves the swap surface weekly. S3, R2, or Supabase Storage slot in behind the same signatures; the rate-limit counters move to Postgres or the host's KV. Email is even cleaner: sendMail() writes the outbox row first and delivers through a raw-fetch adapter — no Resend SDK exists in the dependency tree — so SES, Postmark, or bare SMTP is one new adapter file, invisible to every module.

Payments — designed neutral before a single rupee moves

Payments is the one seam priced before its first implementation: the design was held (migration 051 reserved) pending Razorpay keys, which forced the schema to be provider-agnostic from day one.

The provider-neutral pattern

What exists in code today: the payment_ref column on sales, the pending_payment FSM states in both POS and Booking, and a live HMAC-verified webhook handler on the booking path. None of it says "Razorpay" to the modules. Razorpay is simply the India-first rail; Stripe is the same shape for non-India clients — an adapter and a webhook route. The eventual model is dual-rail per client: a workspace setting selects which provider a tenant's checkout uses, two adapter files behind one seam. Days per rail, not a re-architecture — because the seam was priced while it was still on paper.

Frontend — the one honest "wouldn't port cheaply"

A credible exit inventory needs at least one expensive row, or it's advertising.

The SSR/SEO trade-off

The frontend locks us to React and nothing else — no meta-framework, no proprietary data layer, and Vite is replaceable build tooling. The genuine limit: the public surfaces (storefront, catalog, brand sites) render client-side. For direct links, QR menus, and link-in-bio traffic — how these surfaces are actually used — that's fine. For organic search, it's weak: crawlers do better with server-rendered HTML. If a client's business depends on storefront SEO, the public pages move to Next or Remix — weeks of work, though contained: the internal app has no SEO need and stays a SPA, and the components those pages render are already shared. Known, accepted, priced — like every other row in this chapter. We don't promise every stack; we promise a priced exit from every dependency.

Glossary

Terms that show up throughout this document, defined in one place.

tenant / client / workspace
Three names for the same thing: one business inside ExSol. Has a unique slug (URL identifier) and a name. All their data is scoped to their client_id.
module
A self-contained area of business functionality (POS, Booking, Files, etc.). Each module has its own database tables, permission keys, backend functions, and frontend pages.
product (in registry context)
A purchasable unit that unlocks one or more modules. Confusing word — the same English word also refers to physical things in the catalog. In the registry context, "product" = "what you buy to enable this module."
bucket
A category of data within a module that can be CRUD'd. "Customers" is a bucket of the Booking module; "Employees" is another. Permission keys take the form module.bucket.verb.
L1 / L2 / L3
Levels within a workspace. L1 is the Primary Owner; L2 is one step below (managers); L3 below that (staff). The level hierarchy is used for both permissions and reporting structure (parent_id on user_nodes).
bucket-user
A user_node that has login credentials (Owner, staff). Distinguished from admins (cross-workspace) and customers (no login).
JWT
JSON Web Token. A signed string that carries the bucket-user's identity (sub, email, client_id). Stored in a cookie called bu_session.
FSM
Finite State Machine. A formal model of "states + legal transitions." Used for sales (5 states), bookings (6 states), purchase orders (4 states).
GIST exclusion constraint
A PostgreSQL feature that prevents overlap on a range column. Used by bookings to atomically prevent two bookings for the same resource at the same time. Concurrent requests can't both succeed.
idempotency key
A token the client sends with a write request so the server can detect "this is a retry, not a new request." Used by sale-create and booking-create.
migration
A numbered SQL file that changes the database schema. Applied in order, recorded in schema_migrations.
Netlify Function
A short-lived serverless process that handles one HTTP request. We have 138 of them. Each lives in a .ts file in netlify/functions/.
Depth phase
The build phase after "width" (all 13 ERP modules existing): deepening modules one slice at a time — timesheets onto Workforce, leads onto CRM, and so on. Depth migrations draw from reserved per-module number ranges (063–139).
Reference layer (docs/reference/)
The generated inventories — endpoints, permissions, schema — derived from the code by npm run docs:reference, plus the module-conformance matrix. Regenerated on demand, so they can't rot the way hand-written tables do.
Outbox (email_outbox)
The table that records every email the platform tried to send, written before delivery is attempted. Status sent / logged (no provider key configured — dev mode) / failed. The pattern means "did we email the customer?" is a SQL query, not a guess.
Movements ledger (stock_movements)
The append-only table where every stock change is recorded as a signed, typed row (sale / purchase / adjustment / production / transfer). Multiple modules write through it; inventory_stock is the cached rollup.
Neon
The PostgreSQL hosting service we use. Supports "branching" — the dev and prod databases are conceptually two branches of the same database, with the same schema but different data.
Sibling chat
One of several Claude Code chats running in parallel on different feature branches. The "main chat" (this one) owns integration + deployment. POS / Booking / Login+AMS / Product Manager / File Manager each have their own sibling chat owning their respective modules.
Handoff doc
A markdown file at docs/superpowers/handoffs/ that summarizes "where I left off" so the next agent (or next-day-me) can continue work. The convention is: sections marked OVERWRITE get rewritten each milestone; sections marked APPEND grow over time.
